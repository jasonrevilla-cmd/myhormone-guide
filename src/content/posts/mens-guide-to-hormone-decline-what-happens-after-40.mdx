# Men's Guide to Hormone Decline: What Happens After 40

You're not imagining it. The fatigue that used to lift after a good night's sleep now lingers into the afternoon. The muscle that came easily in your thirties is harder to build and easier to lose. Your drive — in the gym, at work, in the bedroom — feels like it's operating at about seventy percent. Men's hormone decline after 40 is a measurable, physiological reality, and yet millions of men are told it's just stress, just aging, just something to accept.

It isn't something you have to simply accept. This guide breaks down exactly what happens to male hormones after 40, why the symptoms show up the way they do, and what modern medicine actually offers men who want to address the root cause rather than white-knuckle through the next two decades.

## Understanding Men's Hormone Decline After 40: The Biological Basics

The story of male hormone decline starts earlier than most men realize. Testosterone — the primary androgen responsible for muscle mass, energy, sexual function, mood regulation, bone density, and cognitive sharpness — begins declining around age 30. Research published in the Journal of Clinical Endocrinology and Metabolism has consistently shown a decline rate of approximately 1–2% per year. That rate seems small in isolation, but by the time a man reaches 45, he may be operating with 15–30% less testosterone than he had in his mid-twenties.

Testosterone doesn't act alone. Two other hormones compound the picture significantly. Sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) tends to increase with age, binding to available testosterone and making it biologically unavailable. This means even a man with a seemingly "normal" total testosterone reading may have low *free* testosterone — the fraction the body can actually use. Simultaneously, the enzyme aromatase becomes more active in aging men, converting testosterone into estradiol (estrogen). The result is a hormonal environment that feels increasingly unfamiliar: lower available testosterone, higher relative estrogen, and downstream effects on virtually every system in the body.

This is the biological terrain that makes men's hormone decline after 40 more complex than a single number on a lab report.

## What Is Andropause — And Why Does It Go Undiagnosed?

Andropause — sometimes called late-onset hypogonadism or, colloquially, male menopause — is the clinical term for the constellation of symptoms that arise from declining testosterone and related hormonal shifts in aging men. The Endocrine Society and the American Urological Association both recognize it as a real and treatable condition.

Unlike female menopause, which typically unfolds over a defined window of a few years with pronounced, hard-to-ignore symptoms, andropause is an exercise in slow erosion. There is no single moment when everything changes. Instead, the decline is gradual enough that men often rationalize each symptom individually: the fatigue is from a busy schedule, the weight gain is from less gym time, the mood dip is from work stress. By the time a man connects the dots, he may have been living with suboptimal hormones for five to ten years.

This is precisely why andropause symptoms in men are dramatically underdiagnosed. General practitioners, trained to look for acute pathology, may order a standard testosterone panel and declare the results "within normal range" — a range so wide it fails to account for what's actually optimal for a given individual. Many men whose testosterone levels fall in the lower half of the "normal" range report significant quality-of-life impairments. The conventional lab range and the optimal range are not always the same thing, and a growing number of integrative and functional medicine providers are drawing that distinction in clinical practice.

## The Full Spectrum of Andropause Symptoms in Men

Because testosterone affects so many body systems, male hormone decline produces a wide range of symptoms that don't obviously cluster around a single cause. That breadth is part of what makes it so easy to miss.

**Physical symptoms** are often the most immediately recognizable. Reduced muscle mass and strength, increased body fat — particularly visceral fat around the abdomen — decreased bone density, joint aches, reduced exercise recovery, and low energy are hallmarks. Some men also notice hair thinning, reduced beard growth, or changes in skin texture.

**Sexual and reproductive symptoms** include reduced libido, erectile dysfunction or difficulty maintaining erections, decreased ejaculatory volume, and reduced morning erections — a particularly useful clinical marker that many providers ask about specifically.

**Cognitive and psychological symptoms** are frequently the most distressing and the least likely to be attributed to hormones. Brain fog, difficulty concentrating, reduced motivation and drive, increased irritability, anxiety, and a flattened emotional baseline are all commonly reported. Research suggests testosterone plays a direct role in dopamine signaling, which helps explain why declining levels affect mood and reward-seeking behavior.

**Metabolic symptoms** round out the picture: insulin resistance often worsens as testosterone drops, increasing risk factors for type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease. Sleep quality frequently declines, partly because testosterone is closely tied to sleep architecture and partly because low T can worsen sleep apnea.

## How Testosterone Decline by Age Actually Looks: A Reference Guide

Understanding testosterone decline by age in real numbers helps put individual lab results in perspective. The following ranges reflect published data from population studies and clinical reference ranges, with important caveats noted.

| Age Range | Typical Total Testosterone Range (ng/dL) | Common Clinical Observations |
|-----------|------------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| 20–29 | 600–900 | Peak production; high free testosterone |
| 30–39 | 500–800 | Decline begins; often asymptomatic |
| 40–49 | 400–700 | Many men enter symptomatic range |
| 50–59 | 300–600 | Andropause symptoms more prevalent |
| 60–69 | 250–500 | Significant portion of men clinically low |
| 70+ | 200–450 | Decline continues; compounding health risks |

**Important caveats:** These are population averages, not targets. A man at 420 ng/dL who feels terrible may benefit from evaluation just as much as a man at 280 ng/dL. Free testosterone, SHBG, estradiol, LH, FSH, thyroid function, and cortisol all contribute to the complete hormonal picture. Symptoms always matter alongside numbers.

It's also worth noting that "normal" laboratory ranges are typically derived from large population samples — meaning they include many symptomatic men. The range describes what's *common*, not what's *optimal*.

## What to Do When You Recognize the Signs

Recognizing male hormone decline is the first step. The second is understanding your options — and they are more varied and more sophisticated than they were even a decade ago.

**Lab testing is the foundation.** A comprehensive male hormone panel should include total testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, estradiol (E2), LH, FSH, prolactin, thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4), and a metabolic panel. Some providers also run DHEA-S and cortisol. Morning testing is standard, as testosterone levels are highest earlier in the day. Two separate tests are often recommended before any treatment decisions are made.

**Lifestyle optimization matters — but has limits.** Resistance training, sleep quality, stress reduction, reducing alcohol intake, and maintaining healthy body weight all support testosterone production and are worth prioritizing regardless of treatment decisions. Research suggests that significant visceral adiposity (abdominal fat) accelerates aromatase activity, worsening the testosterone-to-estrogen ratio. However, for men with clinically low levels, lifestyle changes alone rarely restore hormones to a range that meaningfully resolves symptoms.

**Bioidentical hormone replacement therapy (BHRT) and testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) are the primary clinical interventions.** Both aim to restore testosterone to an optimal range and alleviate symptoms. They differ meaningfully in approach, delivery methods, and philosophy. If you're weighing your options, [TRT vs. BHRT for Men: What's the Difference?](/trt-vs-bhrt-men) provides a clear, side-by-side breakdown of how the two approaches compare.

One delivery method that has gained significant traction among men pursuing BHRT is testosterone pellet therapy — small, subcutaneous pellets inserted every three to six months that deliver steady, consistent hormone levels without the fluctuations associated with weekly injections or daily gels. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### At what age does testosterone start declining in men?

Testosterone levels in men typically begin a gradual decline around age 30, dropping approximately 1–2% per year. By age 40, many men have measurably lower levels than they did in their twenties. The decline continues through the forties, fifties, and beyond. Some men experience significant symptoms in their early forties, while others don't notice changes until their mid-fifties — individual variation is wide, which is why lab testing matters more than age alone.

### What are the first signs of hormone decline in men over 40?

The earliest signs of male hormone decline are often subtle and easy to dismiss. Fatigue that sleep doesn't fix, a noticeable drop in motivation or drive, reduced libido, and difficulty building or maintaining muscle mass are among the most common early indicators. Brain fog, mild mood changes, and increased body fat — particularly around the abdomen — are also frequently reported. Because these symptoms overlap with stress and normal aging, many men don't connect them to hormones for years.

### Is andropause a real medical condition?

Yes, andropause — sometimes called male menopause or late-onset hypogonadism — is a recognized clinical phenomenon. Unlike female menopause, which involves a sharp hormonal drop over a few years, andropause is a slow, gradual decline that unfolds over decades. The Endocrine Society and other major medical bodies acknowledge that low testosterone in aging men produces real, measurable symptoms. The condition is underdiagnosed largely because the symptoms develop slowly and are frequently attributed to stress or lifestyle factors.

### How is low testosterone diagnosed in men?

Low testosterone is diagnosed through blood testing, typically measuring total testosterone, free testosterone, and sometimes sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG). Testing is ideally done in the morning when levels are at their peak. Most conventional labs flag levels below 300 ng/dL as low, but many functional and integrative providers consider levels below 400–500 ng/dL worth evaluating in the context of symptoms. A single test is rarely enough — patterns, symptoms, and other hormone markers all factor into a complete picture.

### What is the difference between TRT and BHRT for men?

TRT (testosterone replacement therapy) typically refers to pharmaceutical testosterone delivered via injections, gels, or patches, and focuses primarily on restoring testosterone levels. BHRT (bioidentical hormone replacement therapy) uses testosterone that is molecularly identical to what the body produces and may also address other hormones like DHEA, thyroid, and cortisol simultaneously. Both approaches aim to relieve symptoms of male hormone decline, but BHRT tends toward a more comprehensive, personalized protocol. The right choice depends on your symptoms, lab results, and provider.
