# Biote Pellets Explained: How This Popular BHRT System Works, What It Costs, and What to Expect

You've done the research. You've read about hormone pellet therapy, maybe even mentioned it to your doctor — only to have your concerns brushed aside or your symptoms attributed to "just getting older." If you're looking for a straight answer about biote pellets specifically — one of the most widely used pellet-based hormone therapy systems in the country — you're in the right place.

This post breaks down exactly how Biote hormone pellet therapy works, what the insertion procedure involves, how much it realistically costs, and what patients commonly experience along the way. No fluff, no fear-mongering — just the information you need to have an informed conversation with a provider.

## What Are Biote Hormone Pellets and How Does the System Work?

Biote hormone pellets are small, rice-sized cylinders of compounded bioidentical hormones — typically testosterone and/or estradiol — that are inserted under the skin and dissolve gradually over several months. Biote Medical is a Texas-based company that trains and certifies healthcare providers across the United States in a standardized dosing and monitoring protocol, which is one reason the brand name has become so recognizable in the BHRT space.

Unlike oral pills, patches, or topical creams that deliver hormones in waves — causing spikes and dips throughout the day — pellets release hormones steadily and continuously in response to your body's cardiac output. When you exercise or experience stress, blood flow increases and the pellet releases slightly more hormone. When you're at rest, release slows. This physiologic delivery mechanism is one of the core reasons many patients and providers prefer pellets over other formats.

The hormones used in Biote pellets are bioidentical, meaning their molecular structure is identical to the hormones your body naturally produces. Biote works with a network of FDA-registered compounding pharmacies that manufacture pellets to specific dosing specifications. If you want to understand the underlying science of how this delivery mechanism compares to alternatives, [How Pellet Therapy Works: The Science Behind It](/how-pellet-therapy-works) is an excellent deep dive.

## Finding a Biote-Certified Provider: What That Credential Actually Means

A Biote-certified provider is a licensed healthcare professional — typically a physician, nurse practitioner, or physician assistant — who has completed Biote Medical's training program in their standardized hormone optimization protocol. As of recent estimates, Biote has trained more than 7,000 practitioners across the United States, making it one of the largest pellet therapy networks in the country.

The Biote certification process covers comprehensive lab interpretation, individualized dosing calculations, proper pellet insertion technique, and patient follow-up protocols. This standardization matters because one of the most common complaints about BHRT in general is inconsistent dosing — providers using wildly different approaches with no shared framework. Biote's model attempts to address that by anchoring every provider to the same evidence-informed methodology.

To find a Biote-certified provider, patients can use the provider locator on the Biote Medical website. When evaluating a clinic, it's reasonable to ask how many pellet insertions the provider performs per month, whether they conduct comprehensive baseline labs before starting therapy, and what their follow-up monitoring schedule looks like. A thorough provider will run a full hormone panel — including total and free testosterone, estradiol, DHEA-S, thyroid markers, and a metabolic panel — before writing a single pellet order.

## The Biote Pellet Insertion Procedure: A Step-by-Step Overview

The biote pellet insertion is a brief, in-office procedure that typically takes 15 minutes or less from start to finish. Here is what most patients experience:

**Before the appointment:** Your provider reviews your lab results and calculates your personalized pellet dose. For women, dosing is based on symptoms, lab values, weight, and activity level. For men, the calculation also accounts for lean muscle mass and lifestyle factors.

**At the appointment:** You'll lie face-down on the exam table. The most common insertion site is the upper outer quadrant of the buttock, where subcutaneous fat provides natural cushioning and concealment. The area is cleaned and a small amount of local anesthetic — typically lidocaine — is injected to numb the site.

**The insertion:** Once numb, your provider makes a very small incision (roughly 3–4 mm) using a scalpel or trocar. The pellet or pellets are inserted through this opening into the subcutaneous tissue using a specialized insertion device. The incision is then closed with a Steri-Strip or small bandage — no stitches required.

**Post-procedure:** Most patients resume normal activities immediately. Providers typically advise avoiding submersion in water (pools, hot tubs, baths) for 5 to 7 days and limiting strenuous lower-body exercise for 3 to 5 days to allow the insertion site to seal properly. Bruising and mild soreness are normal and typically resolve within a week.

Most patients begin noticing hormonal changes within 2 to 4 weeks. Full optimization — the point at which your levels stabilize and symptoms respond most completely — often occurs after the second or third insertion cycle as your provider fine-tunes your dose.

## How Much Does Biote Pellet Therapy Cost?

Biote pellet therapy cost typically ranges from $300 to $500 per insertion for women and $600 to $900 per insertion for men. The higher cost for men reflects the significantly larger hormone doses required to reach therapeutic testosterone levels in male physiology.

Because most patients require two to four insertions per year, the annual out-of-pocket investment generally falls in the following ranges:

| Patient Type | Per Insertion | Insertions Per Year | Estimated Annual Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Women | $300–$500 | 2–4 | $600–$2,000 |
| Men | $600–$900 | 2–3 | $1,200–$2,700 |

These figures do not include the cost of initial and follow-up lab work, which can range from $100 to $300 per draw depending on your location, provider, and insurance coverage. Some clinics bundle labs into a membership or annual fee; others bill separately.

Insurance coverage for BHRT is limited. Most major insurers classify pellet therapy as elective or experimental and do not reimburse it. However, many patients successfully use Health Savings Accounts (HSA) or Flexible Spending Accounts (FSA) to pay for both the insertion and associated labs — check with your plan administrator for eligibility.

It's worth noting that Biote is not the only pellet system on the market. ## What Results Do Biote Patients Typically Report?

Many patients who complete the full optimization process with Biote hormone pellets report meaningful improvements across a range of symptoms. The most commonly cited benefits include reduced fatigue and improved energy levels, better sleep quality, sharper mental clarity and reduced brain fog, improved mood stability, increased libido, and — particularly in men — improved body composition and muscle maintenance.

Research supports the plausibility of these outcomes. Studies published in peer-reviewed journals have found that subcutaneous hormone pellets produce stable, physiologic serum hormone levels and are associated with improvements in quality-of-life measures including sexual function, mood, and cognitive performance. A study published in *Maturitas* examining testosterone pellet therapy in postmenopausal women found significant improvements in sexual function and well-being scores compared to baseline.

It's important to set realistic expectations. Pellet therapy is not an overnight transformation. The first insertion cycle is often a calibration period — your provider is establishing your baseline response and may adjust dosing at the next insertion. Many patients describe the second and third cycles as the point where they feel "like themselves again." Patience and consistent follow-up with your provider are essential components of success.

It's also worth noting that pellets work best as part of a broader hormone optimization strategy. Sleep, nutrition, stress management, and exercise all influence how effectively your body utilizes hormones. A good Biote-certified provider will address lifestyle factors alongside your pellet protocol.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long do Biote pellets last?

Biote pellets typically last 3 to 6 months for women and 4 to 6 months for men. Absorption rate varies based on your activity level, metabolism, hormone deficiency, and stress levels. Your Biote-certified provider will monitor your labs and schedule your next insertion before levels drop significantly, helping you maintain consistent symptom relief without the peaks and valleys of other delivery methods.

### How much does Biote pellet therapy cost?

Biote pellet therapy typically costs between $300 and $500 per insertion for women and $600 to $900 per insertion for men, who require larger pellet doses. Most patients need 2 to 4 insertions per year. Because BHRT is often categorized as elective, most insurance plans do not cover it, though some HSA and FSA accounts may be used depending on your plan.

### Is Biote pellet therapy FDA approved?

The pellets used in Biote therapy are compounded by FDA-registered pharmacies, but the individual pellets themselves are not FDA-approved drug products. Biote does operate under strict quality and dosing standards, and many of the hormones used — testosterone and estradiol — are FDA-approved in other forms. Patients should discuss the regulatory distinction with their provider before starting therapy.

### What are the side effects of Biote hormone pellets?

Common side effects of Biote hormone pellets include temporary bruising or soreness at the insertion site, mild hair thinning or acne in some women receiving testosterone, and a brief adjustment period of 2 to 4 weeks as your body acclimates to rising hormone levels. Serious adverse events are uncommon but can include infection at the insertion site or pellet extrusion. Discuss your personal risk profile with a certified provider.

## Common Questions About Biote Pellet Therapy

### What's the difference between Biote pellets and regular hormone replacement therapy?

Biote pellets deliver bioidentical hormones — chemically identical to those your body produces — through a subcutaneous implant that dissolves gradually over months. Traditional HRT often uses synthetic hormones delivered via pills or patches that create daily hormonal peaks and troughs. Pellets aim to mimic the body's natural, steady hormone release more closely than most other delivery formats.

### Do the pellets hurt going in?

Most patients report minimal discomfort during the biote pellet insertion because the area is numbed with a local anesthetic before the procedure begins. You may feel pressure during the insertion, and the site can be sore or bruised for a few days afterward. The majority of patients describe the procedure as much easier than they anticipated.

### Can men use Biote hormone pellets, or is it only for women?

Biote pellet therapy is used by both men and women. Men typically receive testosterone pellets to address symptoms of andropause — the gradual decline in testosterone associated with aging — including fatigue, reduced muscle mass, low libido, and mood changes. Men generally require higher doses than women, which is reflected in the higher per-insertion cost.

### How do I know if my Biote pellet dose is correct?

Your provider will check your hormone levels via blood work approximately 4 to 6 weeks after your first insertion to confirm that levels are in the therapeutic range and that symptoms are responding appropriately. Dose adjustments are made at subsequent insertions based on your lab results and how you're feeling. It often takes two or three insertion cycles to land on your optimal dose.

### Can I stop Biote pellet therapy if I decide it's not for me?

Yes. Because pellets dissolve naturally, there is no surgical removal required if you decide to stop. You would simply not schedule your next insertion, and your hormone levels would gradually return to their pre-therapy baseline as the current pellet fully absorbs — typically over 3 to 6 months. Your provider can help you manage any transition symptoms during that period.
