# How to Choose the Right BHRT Delivery Method for You

You know something is off — the fatigue that sleep doesn't fix, the brain fog, the weight that moved in without an invitation. You've done enough research to know that bioidentical hormone replacement therapy might help. But now you're staring down a bewildering list of options — pellets, creams, patches, injections, troches — and wondering how to choose a BHRT delivery method that's actually right for *you*, not just the one your provider happens to offer. That question deserves a real answer.

This guide breaks down every major BHRT delivery method, explains the clinical differences between them, and gives you a practical framework for choosing the best fit based on your symptoms, lifestyle, and health history. By the end, you'll know exactly what to discuss with your provider — and why the "best" method is the one built around your biology.

## How to Choose a BHRT Delivery Method: The Framework That Actually Works

Choosing a BHRT delivery method isn't about picking the trendiest option or the one your friend swears by — it's a clinical decision shaped by four key variables: your hormone levels, your symptom pattern, your lifestyle, and your medical history.

**Your hormone levels** tell your provider how significant your deficiency is and which hormones need to be replaced. A woman with severely depleted testosterone, for example, may need pellet therapy to achieve therapeutic dosing that creams can't consistently deliver. A man with moderate testosterone decline might do well with injections or pellets depending on his preference for convenience versus control.

**Your symptom pattern** matters because some symptoms respond better to steady, consistent hormone delivery while others benefit from flexibility. Constant symptoms — fatigue every day, persistent brain fog, ongoing hot flashes — often improve most with methods that maintain stable blood levels around the clock, like pellets or patches. Cyclical or intermittent symptoms may call for adjustable dosing.

**Your lifestyle** is the practical layer most guides skip. The most effective BHRT method is the one you will actually use consistently. If you travel frequently, forget daily medications, or hate the idea of weekly self-injection, those facts matter as much as clinical considerations.

**Your medical history** can rule certain methods in or out entirely. As we'll cover below, oral estrogen carries a meaningfully different risk profile than transdermal delivery — and that difference isn't trivial.

## Understanding Each BHRT Delivery Method Before You Choose

Each delivery method has a distinct mechanism, and understanding how hormones enter your body matters for both efficacy and safety.

**Pellets** are small, rice-sized cylinders — typically compounded from bioidentical estradiol or testosterone — that are inserted under the skin of the hip or buttock during a brief in-office procedure. They dissolve gradually over three to six months, releasing hormones in response to physical activity and metabolic demand. This mimics the natural pulsatile secretion of the ovaries more closely than any other method. Many patients and providers consider pellets the gold standard for stable, hands-off hormone delivery.

**Transdermal creams and gels** are applied daily to thin-skinned areas like the inner wrist, inner arm, or inner thigh. They absorb through the skin and bypass the liver's first-pass metabolism, which is a significant safety advantage over oral delivery. Dosing is flexible and can be adjusted easily. The main limitation is absorption variability — factors like skin thickness, hydration, and application site can affect how much hormone actually reaches the bloodstream.

**Patches** work similarly to creams but deliver hormones through a reservoir attached to the skin, changed every one to three days depending on the product. Several FDA-approved estradiol patches exist, making this one of the few transdermal options with a full regulatory track record. Patches offer consistent absorption with less daily effort than creams, though some patients experience skin irritation at the application site.

**Injections** deliver hormones — most commonly testosterone — directly into muscle tissue, bypassing the gut and liver entirely. They achieve high bioavailability but typically require weekly or bi-weekly administration, and the peaks and troughs between injections can cause noticeable energy and mood swings in sensitive individuals.

**Oral tablets and troches** (sublingual or buccal lozenges) are the most familiar format but carry the most significant caveats. Oral estrogen undergoes first-pass liver metabolism, which research has linked to increased clotting factor production and elevated cardiovascular risk compared to transdermal options. Troches dissolve under the tongue or against the cheek, partially bypassing the liver, but absorption varies considerably.

## The Safety Difference Between Oral and Transdermal BHRT

Transdermal BHRT delivery methods carry a meaningfully lower risk of blood clots than oral estrogen, and this distinction matters for your decision.

This isn't a minor footnote. A significant body of research — including observational data reviewed by the North American Menopause Society — has found that oral estrogen increases the hepatic production of clotting factors because it passes through the liver before entering systemic circulation. Transdermal estrogen, whether delivered via patch, cream, pellet, or injection, bypasses this first-pass effect entirely.

A landmark study published in *Circulation* found that postmenopausal women using oral hormone therapy had a significantly higher risk of venous thromboembolism compared to those using transdermal delivery. The NAMS 2022 Hormone Therapy Position Statement reinforces that transdermal estrogen is generally preferred for women with elevated cardiovascular risk factors or a history of migraine with aura.

This doesn't mean oral estrogen is appropriate for no one — many women use it safely — but it's a reason why most current evidence-based guidelines lean toward transdermal delivery as the first-line route when there is no specific reason to prefer oral. If your provider recommends oral estrogen, it's worth asking about transdermal alternatives and discussing your individual clot risk profile.

## How Your Lifestyle Should Influence Which BHRT Method You Choose

Your lifestyle is a legitimate clinical variable, not a secondary concern. Adherence is the single biggest predictor of whether any therapy works, and the most elegantly formulated hormone cream is worthless if it sits unused on your bathroom counter.

Ask yourself honestly: Do you consistently take daily medications? If your track record with daily adherence is poor, pellets or a long-acting method deserve serious consideration. The every-3-to-6-month insertion schedule removes daily decision fatigue entirely.

Do you travel frequently? Pellets require nothing but a brief procedure. Creams require measuring and applying daily, patches need refrigeration in some cases, and injectable testosterone has considerations around needles and travel security.

Do you want the ability to stop quickly? Pellets cannot be removed once inserted and will take several months to metabolize. If you want maximum flexibility to pause or stop therapy — for any reason, including an upcoming surgery or a change in diagnosis — topical or patch methods give you that option immediately.

Do you have a caregiver or partner who could be affected by cream transfer? Testosterone cream applied to the skin can transfer to children or partners through skin contact. Pellets and patches eliminate this risk entirely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best BHRT delivery method for menopause symptoms?

There is no single best BHRT delivery method for everyone — the right choice depends on your specific symptoms, lifestyle, health history, and dosing needs. Pellets offer steady hormone levels ideal for persistent fatigue, brain fog, and low libido. Patches and creams work well for women who want adjustable dosing. Research suggests transdermal methods carry a lower blood clot risk than oral delivery. A qualified provider can help you match method to symptoms.

### How do BHRT pellets compare to creams and patches?

BHRT pellets are inserted under the skin every 3 to 6 months and deliver a consistent hormone level without daily effort. Creams and patches require daily or weekly application but allow for easier dose adjustments. Pellets tend to produce the most stable hormone levels, which many patients find reduces symptom fluctuation. Creams offer more flexibility and are easier to stop if needed. The best choice depends on your priorities around convenience, control, and consistency.

### Are BHRT injections better than pellets?

BHRT injections and pellets both bypass the digestive system and deliver hormones directly into the bloodstream, but they differ significantly in dosing rhythm. Injections are typically administered weekly or bi-weekly and can cause peaks and troughs in hormone levels, which some patients experience as mood swings or energy fluctuations. Pellets release hormones gradually over months, producing more stable levels. Many providers prefer pellets for testosterone therapy in both men and women for this reason.

### Can I switch BHRT delivery methods if the first one doesn't work?

Yes — switching BHRT delivery methods is common and often part of the optimization process. Most experienced providers expect some adjustment period and will reassess your delivery method at your follow-up lab review, typically 90 days after starting. Switching from a cream to a patch, or from injections to pellets, is straightforward. The exception is pellets, which cannot be removed once inserted, so if a pellet dose is too high, you would need to wait for it to metabolize before switching.

## Common Questions About Choosing a BHRT Delivery Method

### Which BHRT delivery method is easiest to use?

Pellets are widely considered the most convenient option because they require only one in-office procedure every three to six months — no daily application or weekly injections. After insertion, you simply go about your life. Creams and gels require daily application, and patches need to be changed every one to three days.

### Is there a BHRT method that doesn't require daily effort?

Yes — hormone pellets are the primary low-maintenance option, requiring reinsertion only three to four times per year. Long-acting injectable formulations can also reduce frequency to every one to two weeks. If daily effort is a concern, discuss pellet therapy specifically with your provider.

### Does the BHRT delivery method affect how quickly it works?

Yes, the delivery method influences onset timing. Pellets typically take two to four weeks to reach optimal levels as the body adjusts to gradual release. Injections reach peak levels within 24 to 72 hours but then decline. Patches and creams usually establish steady-state levels within one to two weeks of consistent use.

### Can men use BHRT pellets, or are they only for women?

Men can absolutely use hormone pellets — testosterone pellet therapy is one of the most common applications in men with low testosterone (hypogonadism). Male pellets are typically larger and dosed higher than those used in women, and they are reinserted every four to six months. Studies suggest pellets produce more stable testosterone levels in men than weekly injections.

### Does insurance cover BHRT delivery methods like pellets or creams?

Coverage varies significantly by insurer and by the specific product. FDA-approved transdermal patches and some testosterone injections may be covered under standard prescription benefits. Compounded pellets and custom creams are generally not covered by insurance and are paid out of pocket. Ask your provider for a cost estimate and check with your insurer about coverage for any FDA-approved alternatives before starting therapy.
