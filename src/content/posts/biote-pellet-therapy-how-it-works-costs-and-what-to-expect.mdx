# Biote Pellets: How They Work, What They Cost, and What to Expect Before Your First Insertion

You have done the research. You know your hormones are off. You have probably heard the phrase "biote pellets" mentioned in a Facebook group or from a friend who swears they got their life back after one insertion. But before you book an appointment, you deserve a clear, honest breakdown — not a sales pitch from a provider's website and not a horror story from an anonymous forum. Just the facts.

This guide covers everything you need to know about Biote pellet therapy: the science behind how biote pellets are dosed and inserted, a realistic look at biote pellet cost, what side effects are actually common versus rare, and what your first few months on therapy are likely to feel like. If you are weighing this against other delivery methods or other pellet systems, we have got you covered there too.

## What Biote Pellets Are and How Biote Therapy Actually Works

Biote pellets are small, custom-compounded hormone implants — roughly the size of a grain of rice — inserted subcutaneously into the upper buttock area during an in-office procedure that takes about 15 minutes. The pellets are made from plant-derived hormones that are chemically identical to the estradiol and testosterone your body produces naturally, which is why they fall under the broader category of bioidentical hormone replacement therapy (BHRT).

The Biote Medical company does not manufacture pellets or provide patient care directly. Instead, it operates as a training and practice-management platform: it certifies healthcare providers (primarily OB-GYNs, internists, and nurse practitioners) in its proprietary dosing methodology, supplies them with access to FDA-registered compounding pharmacies, and provides an algorithm-based dosing calculator that uses your lab values, weight, and symptom profile to determine your starting pellet dose.

Once inserted, the pellet dissolves slowly over time, releasing a steady, low-level stream of hormones directly into your bloodstream. This is meaningfully different from pills (which spike and crash through the liver), patches (which can vary with skin absorption and sweating), and injections (which create peaks and troughs many patients find destabilizing). ## How the Biote Dosing Process Works — From Labs to Insertion

Biote therapy begins with comprehensive lab work, not a symptom questionnaire. A Biote-certified provider will typically order a panel that includes total and free testosterone, estradiol, DHEA-S, thyroid markers, a complete metabolic panel, and often a CBC. These numbers feed directly into the Biote dosing algorithm alongside your body weight and symptom severity.

For women, the most common pellets contain testosterone, sometimes combined with estradiol, depending on menopausal status and individual lab findings. For men, testosterone pellets are standard. The Biote system is intentionally conservative with initial doses to allow your body to adjust — which means some patients do not feel optimal after the first pellet and require a dose increase at reinsertion. This is not a failure. It is the expected calibration process, and nearly every provider will tell you that the second or third pellet is when most patients say things click into place.

The insertion itself involves a small incision, a local anesthetic, and a trocar device that places the pellet under the skin. You will be asked to limit strenuous lower-body exercise for a few days afterward to prevent extrusion — the most common procedural complication, where the pellet is expelled before fully integrating. Keeping the insertion site clean and dry and following your provider's post-care instructions dramatically reduces this risk.

## Biote Pellet Cost: What You Should Actually Budget For

Biote pellet therapy costs between $300 and $500 per insertion cycle for women and $500 to $800 per cycle for men at most practices. Those figures are for the pellets themselves. The real annual cost picture is broader, and it is worth building out before you commit.

Here is what a realistic first-year budget looks like:

| Expense | Typical Cost Range |
|---|---|
| Initial consultation | $100–$300 |
| Comprehensive lab panel (initial) | $100–$400 (varies by insurance) |
| First pellet insertion (women) | $300–$500 |
| First pellet insertion (men) | $500–$800 |
| Follow-up lab draw (4–6 weeks) | $75–$200 |
| Subsequent insertions (2–3 per year) | Same as first insertion |
| Biote nutraceuticals (optional) | $50–$150/month |

Insurance almost never covers pellet therapy. Some FSA and HSA plans will reimburse it — ask your clinic for a superbill. The nutraceutical supplements that many Biote-certified providers sell alongside therapy (ADK, DIM, iodine formulas) are genuinely optional, despite sometimes being presented as part of the protocol. ## Biote Pellet Therapy Side Effects: What Is Normal and What to Watch For

The most common side effects of Biote pellets are mild, temporary, and front-loaded in your first 4 to 8 weeks. Knowing what is normal versus what warrants a call to your provider makes the adjustment period far less stressful.

**Common and expected:**
- Insertion-site soreness, bruising, or swelling for 3–7 days
- Increased energy or mild restlessness in the first 1–2 weeks (testosterone surge effect)
- Breast tenderness or light spotting in women during the first cycle
- Mild mood fluctuations as your body recalibrates
- Temporary acne or oily skin, especially in women with higher testosterone doses

**Less common but worth monitoring:**
- Pellet extrusion (the pellet works its way out before dissolving) — occurs in roughly 1–2% of insertions
- Infection at the insertion site — rare with proper sterile technique
- Elevated red blood cell count (polycythemia) in men on testosterone — monitored through follow-up labs
- Hair thinning in women sensitive to androgens — often dose-adjustable

The fear that hormones will make you into a "different person" is one of the most common concerns new patients bring to their first consultation. Research suggests that properly dosed BHRT tends to restore baseline function — patients describe it as feeling like themselves again, not feeling altered. The cognitive restoration piece, what many patients describe as "MY BRAIN WORKS AGAIN," is frequently the first change people notice and tends to appear within the first 2 to 4 weeks.

## Biote vs. Other Pellet Systems: How It Compares

Biote Medical is not the only pellet therapy system on the market. EvexiPEL and SottoPelle are two other major platforms with their own dosing philosophies, training standards, and compounding relationships. All three use bioidentical hormones in pellet form, but they differ in meaningful ways.

| Feature | Biote | EvexiPEL | SottoPelle |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dosing method | Algorithm + weight-based | Symptom + lab-based | Lab-based, lower-dose philosophy |
| Testosterone for women | Yes | Yes | More conservative |
| Network size | Largest U.S. network | Mid-size | Smaller, select providers |
| Nutraceutical program | Proprietary line | No | No |
| Training model | Provider-paid certification | Provider-paid certification | Provider-paid certification |

The right system ultimately depends on your provider's training and your individual hormone profile — not brand loyalty. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long do Biote pellets last?

Biote pellets typically last 3 to 5 months for women and 5 to 6 months for men before re-insertion is needed. The duration depends on your individual hormone metabolism, activity level, stress, and starting hormone levels. Most providers schedule a follow-up lab draw around week 4 to assess how well your body absorbed the pellet and to fine-tune the next insertion dose.

### How much do Biote pellets cost?

Biote pellet therapy typically costs between $300 and $500 per insertion for women and $500 to $800 per insertion for men. Because pellets last 3 to 6 months, annual costs generally range from $600 to $1,600 depending on gender, dosage, and your provider's pricing. Lab work, consultation fees, and nutraceutical supplements sold by the Biote Medical network are usually billed separately.

### What are the side effects of Biote pellet therapy?

Common side effects of Biote pellets include temporary insertion-site soreness, minor bruising, and occasional spotting or breast tenderness in women during the first adjustment period. Some women notice mood shifts or acne at the start, which typically resolve after the first pellet cycle. Men may experience mild fluid retention or changes in mood early on. Serious complications are rare but include pellet extrusion and infection at the insertion site.

### Is Biote therapy covered by insurance?

Biote pellet therapy is rarely covered by insurance and is considered an elective out-of-pocket expense at most practices. A small number of providers offer FSA or HSA billing, so it is worth asking your clinic directly. Conventional HRT alternatives like patches or gels are more commonly reimbursed, which is worth factoring into your total cost comparison.

## Common Questions About Biote Pellet Therapy

### How do I know if I'm a good candidate for Biote pellets?

Most Biote-certified providers evaluate candidacy based on comprehensive lab work combined with your symptom history. Women in perimenopause or menopause with low estradiol or testosterone, and men with clinically low testosterone, are the most common candidates. A thorough initial consultation — not just a symptom quiz — is the right starting point.

### Can I stop Biote pellet therapy if I don't like how I feel?

Unlike a patch or pill you can simply stop taking, pellets cannot be removed once inserted and will continue releasing hormones until they dissolve. If you have a bad reaction to a dose, your provider can sometimes counteract effects with medication, but you generally need to wait out the current cycle. This makes choosing an experienced, conservative provider especially important for your first insertion.

### Will Biote pellets help with weight loss?

Biote pellets are not a weight-loss treatment, but many patients report that restoring optimal testosterone and estradiol levels makes it significantly easier to build lean muscle, reduce stubborn fat, and maintain energy for exercise. Research suggests that low testosterone in both men and women is associated with increased fat mass and reduced muscle mass, so hormone optimization can support — but does not replace — lifestyle changes.

### How is Biote different from regular HRT my OB-GYN might prescribe?

Conventional HRT typically uses FDA-approved, commercially manufactured hormone products in standard doses delivered as pills, patches, or gels. Biote pellets are compounded to a custom dose, delivered subcutaneously for steady-state absorption, and administered through a certified-provider network with a proprietary dosing protocol. The hormones in both approaches can be bioidentical — the key differences are delivery method, dosing philosophy, and the individualization of treatment.

### What happens at my first Biote consultation?

Your first consultation with a Biote-certified provider typically includes a review of your symptoms, a comprehensive hormone lab panel ordered that day or reviewed if you brought recent results, and a discussion of your health history including any cancer risk factors. If you are a candidate, your provider will walk you through the dosing rationale and schedule your insertion appointment — usually within one to two weeks after labs return.
