# BHRT vs. Conventional HRT: What's the Difference?

If you've been researching hormone therapy, you've almost certainly run into the debate over BHRT vs. conventional HRT — and likely walked away more confused than when you started. One side calls bioidentical hormones a revolutionary, natural solution. The other dismisses them as unproven and overhyped. Meanwhile, you're still dealing with hot flashes at 2 a.m., brain fog that makes you feel like a stranger in your own mind, and a body that doesn't feel like yours anymore. You deserve a clear, honest answer — not a sales pitch from either camp.

This post breaks down exactly what separates bioidentical hormone replacement therapy from conventional hormone therapy: the science, the practical differences, the legitimate concerns, and what the research actually shows. By the end, you'll have the foundational knowledge to have a real conversation with your provider — and to cut through the noise you'll find everywhere else online.

## Understanding the Core Difference in BHRT vs. Conventional HRT

The most fundamental distinction between BHRT and conventional HRT comes down to molecular structure. It sounds technical, but it's actually a simple concept once you understand it.

**Bioidentical hormones** are molecules that are structurally identical — atom for atom — to the hormones your body naturally produces. Bioidentical estradiol, for example, has the exact same three-dimensional shape as the estradiol your ovaries make. Your cell receptors, which work like locks, are designed to receive a very specific key. Bioidentical hormones are cut to fit that lock precisely.

**Conventional or synthetic hormones** use molecules that are chemically similar to your natural hormones but not identical. The most well-known example is medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA), a synthetic progestin used in many traditional HRT regimens. Another is conjugated equine estrogens (CEE), derived from the urine of pregnant mares, which contain a mixture of estrogens — some of which are not native to the human body.

The argument for bioidentical hormones is logical on its face: if your body's receptors evolved to work with a specific molecular shape, using that exact shape should produce a cleaner, more predictable biological response. This is the scientific rationale, and it's not without merit — though it's also more nuanced than marketers tend to suggest. ## A Brief History: How We Got Two Different Approaches

Understanding why two separate approaches exist helps you evaluate them more critically.

Conventional HRT rose to prominence in the 1960s and became widely prescribed through the 1980s and 1990s. The dominant products of that era — Premarin (conjugated equine estrogens) and Provera (medroxyprogesterone acetate) — were developed from what was practical and patentable at the time. Natural, bioidentical molecules cannot be patented, which historically made them less commercially attractive for large pharmaceutical manufacturers to develop and study.

In 2002, the Women's Health Initiative (WHI) published results that shook the medical establishment. The study found that women taking a combination of conjugated equine estrogens and medroxyprogesterone acetate had increased risks of breast cancer, heart disease, and stroke compared to women taking a placebo. Hormone therapy prescriptions dropped dramatically almost overnight.

What followed was a critical reassessment. Researchers began asking whether the risks identified in the WHI were tied to *which* hormones were used, at *what doses*, via *which delivery methods*, and in *which patients* — rather than being an inherent property of all hormone therapy. That reassessment is still actively underway, and it's a major reason the BHRT vs. conventional HRT conversation became so medically important. For a detailed look at what the current evidence actually shows, see our article on [BHRT safety and the research behind it](/bhrt-safety-research).

## The Progesterone vs. Progestin Distinction: Why It Matters

Of all the differences between bioidentical and synthetic hormones, the progesterone-versus-progestin distinction is arguably the most clinically significant — and the one most worth understanding.

**Micronized progesterone** (sold under the brand name Prometrium, among others) is bioidentical. It is molecularly identical to the progesterone your ovaries produce during the luteal phase of your cycle.

**Progestins**, such as medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA), are synthetic analogs. They were engineered to prevent uterine lining buildup — which progesterone does naturally — but their different molecular structure means they interact with receptors throughout the body differently.

The WHI study that raised red flags used MPA, not bioidentical progesterone. Subsequent research, including a large French cohort study published in the *International Journal of Cancer*, found that women using estrogen combined with bioidentical progesterone did not show the same elevated breast cancer risk as those using synthetic progestins. Researchers have proposed several mechanisms for this difference, including the fact that MPA has androgenic (testosterone-like) activity that bioidentical progesterone does not.

This does not mean bioidentical progesterone is risk-free or that the question is fully settled — it isn't. But the distinction is real, clinically relevant, and increasingly recognized even within mainstream endocrinology. The Endocrine Society and other major medical bodies have begun acknowledging that not all progestogens carry identical risk profiles.

## FDA-Approved Bioidentical Hormones vs. Compounded BHRT

This is where significant confusion — and legitimate controversy — enters the picture.

Many people don't realize that **FDA-approved bioidentical hormone products already exist** and are widely prescribed. Estradiol patches (like Vivelle-Dot or Climara), estradiol gels and sprays, and oral micronized progesterone (Prometrium) are all bioidentical and have gone through the full FDA approval process, including clinical trials for safety and efficacy. These are available by prescription from any licensed doctor.

**Compounded BHRT** is a separate category. Compounding pharmacies can create custom hormone preparations — specific doses, combinations, or delivery forms not available in commercial products — based on a provider's prescription. Proponents argue that compounding allows for personalized dosing tailored to an individual patient's hormone levels and symptoms.

The FDA has raised concerns about compounded BHRT, primarily because compounded products are not subject to the same pre-market testing requirements as FDA-approved drugs. Quality, potency, and sterility can vary between compounding pharmacies. This doesn't mean compounded BHRT is inherently dangerous, but it does mean the evidentiary bar is different and that the burden falls heavily on provider expertise and pharmacy quality.

## BHRT vs. Conventional HRT: A Side-by-Side Comparison

| Feature | Bioidentical BHRT | Conventional HRT |
|---|---|---|
| **Molecular structure** | Identical to human hormones | Similar but not identical (synthetic) |
| **Common estrogen forms** | Estradiol (E2) | Conjugated equine estrogens (CEE) |
| **Common progesterone forms** | Micronized progesterone | Medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA) |
| **FDA-approved options** | Yes (estradiol products, Prometrium) | Yes (Premarin, Provera, Prempro) |
| **Compounded options available** | Yes | Rarely |
| **Insurance coverage** | Often covered (FDA-approved forms) | Often covered |
| **Personalizable dosing** | Yes, especially compounded forms | Limited |
| **Research volume** | Growing, but less than conventional | Extensive, including large RCTs |
| **WHI study findings** | Not directly studied (used CEE + MPA) | Mixed; elevated risk with CEE + MPA combo |
| **Mainstream medical acceptance** | Increasing for FDA-approved forms | Standard of care |

**Key takeaway:** The binary of "natural BHRT vs. risky synthetic HRT" is an oversimplification in both directions. Several FDA-approved bioidentical options are well-studied and increasingly recommended even by conventional endocrinologists. And not all conventional HRT carries the same risk profile — estrogen-only therapy, for example, showed a different (and more favorable) risk picture in the WHI than the combination therapy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is BHRT safer than conventional HRT?

The safety profiles of BHRT and conventional HRT are still being actively studied, and neither is universally safer than the other. FDA-approved bioidentical hormones like estradiol and progesterone have a well-established safety record. Compounded BHRT, however, lacks the same level of regulatory oversight. Risks and benefits depend heavily on your individual health history, the hormones used, dosage, and delivery method. Always discuss your personal risk profile with a qualified provider.

### What does "bioidentical" actually mean?

Bioidentical means the hormone molecule is structurally identical to the hormone your body naturally produces. For example, bioidentical estradiol has the exact same molecular shape as the estradiol your ovaries make. This is different from synthetic hormones like medroxyprogesterone acetate, which are chemically similar but not identical. The distinction matters because hormone receptors in your cells are designed to respond to a precise molecular shape.

### Can you get bioidentical hormones from a regular doctor?

Yes. Several FDA-approved bioidentical hormone products — including estradiol patches, gels, and sprays, plus oral micronized progesterone — are available by prescription through any licensed physician. You do not need to see a specialized BHRT clinic to access these options. Compounded bioidentical hormones, which are custom-mixed by a compounding pharmacy, require a prescription but are not FDA-approved as finished drug products.

### What is the main difference between progesterone and progestin?

Progesterone is the bioidentical form — molecularly identical to what your ovaries produce. Progestins are synthetic versions, such as medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA), engineered to mimic progesterone's effects but with a different molecular structure. This difference is clinically significant: the landmark Women's Health Initiative study linked MPA to increased breast cancer risk, while subsequent research on bioidentical micronized progesterone suggests a more favorable safety profile, though research is ongoing.

### Does insurance cover BHRT?

FDA-approved bioidentical hormone products — like estradiol patches or oral micronized progesterone — are typically covered by insurance in the same way as any other prescription drug, subject to your plan's formulary and copay structure. Custom-compounded BHRT is usually not covered by insurance because it is not FDA-approved as a finished product. Costs for compounded formulations vary widely depending on the pharmacy and formulation.

### Are all bioidentical hormones the same?

No. "Bioidentical" describes the molecular structure of the hormone, not the product itself. Bioidentical hormones come in two very different categories: FDA-approved commercial products that have undergone rigorous clinical testing, and custom-compounded preparations mixed by a pharmacy to a provider's specifications. These two categories differ significantly in terms of regulatory oversight, quality testing, and evidence base. Choosing between them requires careful discussion with your healthcare provider.
