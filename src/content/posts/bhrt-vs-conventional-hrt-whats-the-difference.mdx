# BHRT vs. Conventional HRT: What's the Difference?

If you've spent any time researching hormone therapy, you've almost certainly run into the debate between BHRT vs. conventional HRT — and come away more confused than when you started. On one side, conventional medicine largely stands behind FDA-approved synthetic hormones with decades of clinical data. On the other, a growing number of patients and providers advocate for bioidentical hormones as a more natural, better-tolerated alternative. Both sides have strong opinions. What's often missing is a clear, honest comparison that respects your intelligence and helps you actually understand the distinction.

That's exactly what this article delivers. We'll break down how bioidentical and synthetic hormones are made, how they differ at the molecular level, what the research says about each, and what questions are worth asking your provider. By the end, you'll have a solid foundation to make a more informed decision — or at least to have a more productive conversation with a clinician who can guide you.

## Understanding the Core Distinction in BHRT vs. Conventional HRT

The debate between BHRT and conventional HRT really comes down to one foundational question: does the molecular structure of a hormone matter once it's inside your body?

**Bioidentical hormones** are compounds whose molecular structure is identical to the hormones your body produces on its own — specifically estradiol, progesterone, and testosterone. Despite being derived from plants (typically wild yam or soy), bioidentical hormones go through extensive processing in a laboratory to achieve that precise molecular match. The word "natural" can be misleading here; bioidentical hormones are not less processed, they're just processed differently. If you want a thorough grounding in what BHRT actually is and how it works, our [complete beginner's guide to BHRT](/what-is-bhrt) is a great place to start.

**Conventional HRT**, by contrast, often uses hormones with structures that differ — sometimes slightly, sometimes significantly — from what your body makes. The most well-known example is conjugated equine estrogens (CEE), sold under the brand name Premarin, which is derived from pregnant mare urine and contains a mixture of estrogens, some of which do not occur naturally in the human body. Another common conventional option is medroxyprogesterone acetate (MPA), a synthetic progestin that mimics some of progesterone's effects but has a distinctly different molecular fingerprint.

The critical question isn't which label sounds more appealing — it's whether these structural differences produce meaningfully different effects on your body's hormone receptors, tissues, and long-term health outcomes. That, as you'll see, is where the science gets genuinely interesting.

## How Bioidentical vs. Synthetic Hormones Are Made

One of the most persistent myths about bioidentical hormones is that they are somehow "straight from nature" — that you could theoretically eat a wild yam and get the same effect. You can't. The precursor compounds in plants like diosgenin must be converted through a multi-step chemical synthesis before they become bioidentical estradiol or progesterone. The "bioidentical" designation refers to the end product's molecular identity with human hormones, not to a simple extraction process.

Conventional synthetic hormones follow a different manufacturing pathway, often designed to create molecules that are hormonally active but patentable — meaning they must be structurally distinct from molecules that occur in nature. This is partly why drugs like Premarin and Provera exist in the forms they do. They work, and they have decades of use behind them, but their structures interact with hormone receptors in ways that differ from your body's native hormones.

FDA-approved bioidentical hormones — including estradiol patches, gels, and sprays, as well as micronized progesterone (brand name Prometrium) — are manufactured by pharmaceutical companies, subject to FDA approval, and held to standardized quality and dosing requirements. These are distinct from **compounded BHRT**, which is prepared by a compounding pharmacy based on a specific prescription. Compounded preparations allow for customized doses and delivery forms but are not FDA-approved as finished drug products, which introduces a different set of considerations around quality control and consistency.

## What the Research Reveals About HRT Differences

No conversation about HRT differences is complete without acknowledging the Women's Health Initiative (WHI), the landmark clinical trial that sent shockwaves through women's health in 2002. The WHI found increased risks of breast cancer, heart disease, stroke, and blood clots among postmenopausal women using a combination of conjugated equine estrogens and medroxyprogesterone acetate. The fallout was enormous — millions of women abruptly stopped hormone therapy, often with no alternative plan for managing debilitating symptoms.

What has emerged in the years since, however, is important nuance. Follow-up analyses from the WHI and other studies have suggested that the risks identified may be more closely associated with the specific formulation tested (CEE plus MPA) than with hormone therapy as a category. Research published in the journal *Climacteric* and other peer-reviewed publications has explored whether micronized progesterone carries a lower breast cancer risk than synthetic progestins like MPA — with some, though not yet definitive, evidence suggesting it does.

Observational studies, including the large French E3N cohort study, found that women using estradiol combined with micronized progesterone had a risk profile that differed favorably from those using synthetic progestins. These findings are promising but not conclusive — observational data has limitations that randomized controlled trials do not. For a comprehensive look at what the evidence actually shows about bioidentical hormone safety, our dedicated article on [BHRT safety research](/bhrt-safety-research) walks through the studies in detail.

The honest summary: the research landscape is evolving, and the blanket dismissal of BHRT that you may encounter from some conventional providers is not fully supported by the current evidence — but neither are the more extravagant marketing claims sometimes made on BHRT's behalf.

## Customization, Delivery, and the Compounding Question

One practical difference that often drives patients toward BHRT is the potential for customization. Standard pharmaceutical hormone products come in fixed doses and a limited range of delivery formats. Compounded BHRT can be tailored — in theory — to your specific lab values and symptom profile, delivered in forms that range from creams and gels to troches (lozenges), sublingual drops, pellets implanted under the skin, and more.

This flexibility is genuinely appealing, particularly for patients who haven't responded well to standard doses or who need a delivery method their body tolerates better. The range of available options is worth understanding in depth — our guide to [BHRT delivery methods compared](/bhrt-delivery-methods-compared) is the most thorough resource we have on this topic.

The tradeoff is oversight. The FDA does not approve compounded preparations as finished drug products, meaning each batch is not independently verified for potency, purity, or consistency in the same way a pharmaceutical product is. Reputable compounding pharmacies voluntarily pursue accreditation through organizations like the Pharmacy Compounding Accreditation Board (PCAB), which provides an important quality signal — but this is not universal. If you pursue compounded BHRT, your provider's relationship with a high-quality, accredited pharmacy matters significantly.

## BHRT vs. Conventional HRT: A Side-by-Side Comparison

The following table summarizes the key differences at a glance. This is a starting point for conversation with your provider — not a substitute for individualized medical guidance.

| Feature | BHRT (Bioidentical) | Conventional HRT (Synthetic) |
|---|---|---|
| **Molecular structure** | Identical to human hormones | May differ from human hormones |
| **Common sources** | Plant-derived (soy, wild yam), lab-processed | Synthetic chemistry; some animal-derived (e.g., equine estrogens) |
| **FDA-approved options** | Yes — estradiol, micronized progesterone | Yes — Premarin, Provera, Prempro, and others |
| **Compounded options** | Yes — customizable but not FDA-approved as finished products | Rarely used in compounded form |
| **Dosing flexibility** | High (especially compounded) | Moderate — fixed pharmaceutical doses |
| **Delivery formats** | Patches, gels, creams, pellets, troches, injections | Pills, patches, rings, creams |
| **Research depth** | Growing; strongest for FDA-approved forms | Extensive; including large RCTs like the WHI |
| **Personalization potential** | High | Moderate |
| **Cost & insurance coverage** | Varies; compounded often not covered | Often covered by insurance |

Neither column is uniformly superior. The right choice depends on your individual physiology, health history, risk factors, and what you and your provider decide together.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the main difference between BHRT and conventional HRT?

BHRT uses hormones that are molecularly identical to those your body produces naturally, derived from plant sources and compounded or manufactured to match human hormones exactly. Conventional HRT typically uses synthetic hormones or hormones derived from animal sources — such as conjugated equine estrogens — whose molecular structure differs from endogenous human hormones. This structural difference is believed by many providers to influence how the hormones interact with receptors in the body, though research is ongoing.

### Is BHRT safer than conventional HRT?

The safety comparison between BHRT and conventional HRT is nuanced. FDA-approved bioidentical hormones like estradiol patches and micronized progesterone have a well-established safety profile. Compounded BHRT carries additional uncertainty because these products are not FDA-approved and lack the same standardized testing. The North American Menopause Society recommends using the lowest effective dose of any hormone therapy for the shortest time needed to manage symptoms. For a deeper look at the evidence, see our article on [BHRT safety research](/bhrt-safety-research).

### Does BHRT require a prescription?

Yes. Whether you choose BHRT or conventional HRT, prescription hormone therapy requires a prescription from a licensed healthcare provider. Compounded BHRT is prescribed by a physician or other qualified provider and prepared by a licensed compounding pharmacy. Over-the-counter products marketed as "natural hormone support" are not the same as prescription BHRT and are not regulated by the FDA as hormone therapies.

### Can men use BHRT?

Absolutely. BHRT is not exclusively a women's treatment. Men experiencing andropause — the gradual decline in testosterone that often begins in their mid-40s — may be candidates for bioidentical testosterone therapy. Bioidentical testosterone is chemically identical to the testosterone the male body produces and is available in several delivery forms including gels, creams, injections, and pellets. As with any hormone therapy, treatment should be guided by lab testing and a qualified provider.

### What are the most common forms of conventional HRT?

The most widely prescribed forms of conventional HRT include conjugated equine estrogens (sold under the brand name Premarin), medroxyprogesterone acetate (a synthetic progestin sold as Provera), and combination products like Prempro. These medications are FDA-approved and have decades of clinical data behind them, including the landmark Women's Health Initiative study. They remain a valid and effective option for many patients managing menopause symptoms.

### How do I know which type of hormone therapy is right for me?

The right type of hormone therapy depends on your individual symptoms, health history, hormone levels, personal preferences, and risk factors. There is no universal answer. A thorough evaluation with a knowledgeable provider — one who reviews your labs, listens to your symptoms, and discusses both options honestly — is the essential first step. Using a structured symptom checklist before your appointment can help you communicate your experience more effectively and lead to a better conversation.
