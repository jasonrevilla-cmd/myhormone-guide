# The Pellet Insertion Procedure: What to Expect Step by Step

You've done your research, talked to your doctor, and decided that pellet therapy might be the right path forward. But now you're wondering: what actually happens during the pellet insertion procedure? What does it feel like? How long will it take? What does recovery look like? These are completely reasonable questions — and the fact that so few providers take the time to answer them thoroughly is one of the most common frustrations patients share. This guide changes that.

Below, you'll find a detailed, honest walkthrough of the entire hormone pellet insertion experience — from the week before your appointment through the first month of recovery. No vague reassurances, no glossing over the parts that feel a little intimidating. Just clear information so you can walk into that appointment feeling prepared and confident.

## What the Pellet Insertion Procedure Actually Is

Before diving into the steps, it helps to have a clear picture of what you're agreeing to. The pellet implant procedure is a minor in-office intervention — not surgery in any meaningful sense of the word, but more involved than a shot or a cream application.

A provider makes a very small incision, typically 3 to 5 millimeters, in the upper outer area of your buttock. Through that incision, one or more compressed hormone pellets — each roughly the size of a grain of rice — are inserted into the subcutaneous fat layer just beneath the skin. The incision is then closed with a sterile adhesive strip or a single suture, and that's it. No general anesthesia. No hospital visit. No significant downtime.

The pellets themselves are made from bioidentical hormones — most commonly testosterone, estradiol, or both — compressed into a solid cylinder. Once implanted, they dissolve slowly over three to six months, releasing hormones directly into the bloodstream at a steady rate. If you want to understand the science behind why this delivery method produces such consistent hormone levels, our guide on [how pellet therapy works](/how-pellet-therapy-works) covers the pharmacokinetics in depth.

The reason this delivery mechanism is gaining traction is straightforward: it eliminates the peaks and valleys that come with daily pills, creams, or weekly injections. Your body gets a consistent hormone supply without you having to remember anything.

## The Week Before Your Appointment: Preparation

Good preparation makes the actual procedure smoother and reduces the small risk of complications. Here's what most providers recommend in the days leading up to your hormone pellet insertion.

**Lab work comes first.** If you haven't already had comprehensive hormone labs done — typically including total and free testosterone, estradiol, DHEA-S, thyroid panel, and a complete metabolic panel — your provider will order these before scheduling your insertion. Your pellet dosing is calculated from these numbers, so this step is non-negotiable. Never let a provider implant pellets without current bloodwork.

**Review your medications.** Certain blood thinners, including daily aspirin, ibuprofen, fish oil supplements, and prescription anticoagulants, can increase bruising and bleeding at the insertion site. Your provider will likely ask you to pause some of these for several days before the procedure. Always confirm this with your provider rather than stopping prescription medications on your own.

**Wear comfortable, loose clothing.** Since the insertion site is your upper buttock, you'll want to wear pants or shorts you can easily lower slightly, and nothing tight against that area. Skip the fitted jeans.

**Eat normally.** Unlike surgical procedures, the pellet insertion doesn't require fasting. Have a regular meal beforehand. Being light-headed from skipping food on top of procedure-day nerves isn't a combination anyone enjoys.

## The Day of the Procedure: A Step-by-Step Walkthrough

Here is exactly what happens during a standard hormone pellet insertion appointment.

**Step 1: Consultation and dose confirmation.**
Before anyone touches you, your provider should review your lab results with you, confirm your dosing rationale, and answer any last questions. This is your moment to voice concerns. A good provider won't rush this part.

**Step 2: Positioning.**
You'll lie face-down on the exam table, or in some cases slightly on your side. The provider will identify and mark the insertion site — almost universally in the upper outer quadrant of the buttock, just below the beltline. This spot is chosen because it has a sufficient layer of subcutaneous fat for consistent absorption and involves minimal movement during healing.

**Step 3: Cleaning and draping.**
The insertion area is cleaned thoroughly with an antiseptic solution, typically betadine or chlorhexidine. A sterile drape may be placed around the site.

**Step 4: Local anesthetic injection.**
This is the part most patients describe as the most noticeable sensation of the entire procedure. A small amount of lidocaine is injected into the skin and underlying tissue to numb the area. You'll feel a brief sting or burn — usually lasting only a few seconds — as the anesthetic takes effect. Most providers wait a minute or two for full numbness before proceeding.

**Step 5: The incision.**
Once the area is numb, the provider makes a tiny incision — again, typically 3 to 5 mm. Most patients feel pressure but not pain at this stage. If you feel anything sharp, tell your provider immediately so they can add more anesthetic.

**Step 6: Pellet insertion.**
Using a sterile trocar (a small hollow instrument designed for this purpose), the provider inserts the pellet or pellets into the subcutaneous fat layer at an angle. The trocar guides the pellets to the correct depth and position. You may feel pressure or a brief pushing sensation, but this should not be painful. Depending on your dosing, you may receive anywhere from one to six pellets in a single session — women typically receive fewer pellets at lower doses, while men receiving testosterone therapy often require more pellets at higher doses.

**Step 7: Closure.**
The incision is closed with a steri-strip adhesive tape or, occasionally, a single dissolving suture. No stitches that need removal in most cases. The site is covered with a small bandage.

**Step 8: Post-procedure instructions.**
Before you leave, your provider should review written aftercare instructions with you. Don't skip this part — the first five to seven days matter for how well the insertion site heals.

The whole appointment, from walking in to walking out, typically runs 30 to 60 minutes. The hands-on portion is closer to 10 to 15 minutes.

## Recovery: The First 7 Days After Hormone Pellet Insertion

The pellet implant procedure has a short and manageable recovery for most patients. That said, what you do — and don't do — in the first week directly affects both healing and how well the pellets seat properly in the tissue.

**Days 1–2:** Some tenderness, mild swelling, and light bruising at the insertion site are completely normal. Keep the bandage dry. Showers are generally permitted after 24 hours; baths, pools, and hot tubs are not. Avoid sitting directly on the insertion site for extended periods.

**Days 3–5:** Most of the acute soreness should be fading. You can typically return to desk work immediately after the procedure, but if your job involves extended sitting that puts pressure on the insertion site, a cushion or brief standing breaks can help. Avoid intense lower-body exercise — squats, running, cycling, heavy lifting — during this window.

**Days 5–7:** The incision should be closing well. Continue avoiding water submersion. Most providers give clearance for normal activity, including lower-body exercise, after day five or seven, depending on healing.

The most important thing to watch for is signs of infection: increasing redness spreading beyond the insertion site, warmth, pus, fever, or pain that's getting worse rather than better. These are not typical and require a call to your provider. ## When Will You Feel the Effects?

This is the question everyone wants answered, and the honest answer is: it varies. Many patients begin noticing early effects — better sleep, a subtle shift in energy — within the first two to four weeks. Full hormonal optimization, however, typically takes four to six weeks as pellet absorption ramps up and your body adjusts to more stable hormone levels.

Some patients, particularly those who were significantly depleted before starting therapy, may take longer to feel the full benefit — sometimes up to three months after their first insertion. This is not a sign the therapy isn't working. It's a sign that your body has a lot of ground to recover. ## Quick-Reference: What to Do and Avoid After the Procedure

| Timeframe | Allowed | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Day 1 | Light activity, desk work, standing | Baths, pools, exercise, direct pressure on site |
| Days 2–4 | Showering (keeping bandage dry), walking | Lower-body exercise, water submersion |
| Days 5–7 | Normal daily activity, light exercise | High-intensity lower-body workouts, hot tubs |
| After Day 7 | Full activity (with provider clearance) | Contact sports if site hasn't fully closed |
| Ongoing | Monitor for infection signs | Ignoring unusual symptoms |

**Contact your provider immediately if you experience:** fever over 101°F, spreading redness, discharge from the insertion site, or pain that worsens after day three rather than improving.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does the pellet insertion procedure hurt?

Most patients describe the pellet insertion procedure as mildly uncomfortable rather than painful. A local anesthetic is injected before the incision, which is the sharpest sensation most people feel. The actual implant placement is typically pressure without significant pain. Some patients report minor soreness at the site for a few days afterward, similar to a bruise. Providers use very small incisions — usually 3–5 mm — so discomfort is generally brief and manageable with over-the-counter pain relievers.

### How long does the hormone pellet insertion appointment take?

The hormone pellet insertion itself takes only 10 to 15 minutes from incision to closure. However, your full appointment will typically run 30 to 60 minutes when you factor in paperwork review, a brief consultation to confirm your dosing based on your labs, the procedure, and post-procedure instructions. You can usually drive yourself home and return to a desk job the same day.

### Where on the body are hormone pellets inserted?

Hormone pellets are almost always inserted in the upper outer quadrant of the buttock — the area just below the beltline on either side. This location is chosen because it has a good layer of subcutaneous fat for consistent hormone absorption, is low-traffic in terms of muscle use, and is easy to keep clean and protected during healing. Some providers may use the hip or lower abdomen in certain cases, but the upper glute is by far the most common site.

### What can't you do after pellet therapy insertion?

For the first five to seven days after the pellet implant procedure, providers typically advise avoiding submersion in water (pools, hot tubs, baths), intense lower-body exercise like squats and running, and direct pressure on the insertion site. Showers are usually fine after 24 to 48 hours. Most patients can resume light activity and desk work immediately. Full activity restrictions depend on your provider's protocol and how quickly the small incision closes.
