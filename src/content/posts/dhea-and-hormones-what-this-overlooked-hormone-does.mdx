# DHEA Hormone Women Need to Know About — The Precursor Everyone Ignores

If you're in perimenopause or menopause and feel like something is missing from the hormone conversation, you might be right. The DHEA hormone women produce naturally is one of the most important — and most overlooked — players in the entire hormonal system. It quietly powers your energy, your libido, your immune resilience, and your sense of self. And it starts declining in your late 20s, long before most women even know it exists.

You've probably heard plenty about estrogen and progesterone. But if no one has talked to you about DHEA yet, you may be missing a critical piece of the puzzle. This post breaks down exactly what DHEA is, what it does, how to recognize when it's low, and what the current research says about supplementation — so you can have a much more informed conversation with your provider.

## What Is DHEA and Why Does It Matter for Women?

DHEA (dehydroepiandrosterone) is a steroid hormone produced primarily by the adrenal glands, with smaller amounts made in the ovaries and brain. It is the most abundant circulating steroid hormone in the human body, and it functions as a prohormone — meaning the body converts it into other active hormones, including both estrogen and testosterone.

Think of DHEA as a hormonal raw material. Your body takes it and manufactures what it needs, where it needs it. This is different from estrogen or testosterone, which act directly on target tissues. DHEA's flexibility is part of what makes it so important, and so complex.

DHEA levels peak in your mid-20s and then decline at a rate of roughly 2–3% per year. By the time a woman reaches her mid-40s, she may have only 50–60% of the DHEA she had at her peak. By her 70s, that number can drop to 10–20%. This steady, decades-long decline runs parallel to — and contributes to — many of the hormonal changes women experience in perimenopause and menopause.

To understand where DHEA fits in the broader picture of female hormones, it helps to first have a foundation in the key players. ## How Low DHEA Affects Women in Perimenopause and Menopause

Low DHEA in women produces a constellation of symptoms that are easy to misattribute to "just getting older" or "stress." Many women report that these changes crept up gradually, which made them harder to recognize as hormonal in origin.

Because DHEA is a precursor to both estrogen and testosterone, its decline affects multiple systems simultaneously. When DHEA drops, so does the reservoir from which your body draws to maintain those downstream hormones. The result can be a compounding hormonal deficit that goes undetected if providers are only testing estradiol and progesterone.

Common signs that DHEA may be low include:

- **Persistent fatigue** that sleep doesn't fully resolve
- **Decreased libido** and reduced sexual satisfaction
- **Vaginal dryness and thinning** (genitourinary syndrome of menopause)
- **Brain fog and difficulty concentrating**
- **Depressed mood or increased anxiety**
- **Thinning hair and dry skin**
- **Difficulty building or maintaining muscle mass**
- **Reduced bone density**
- **More frequent illness** due to weakened immune response

These symptoms overlap significantly with low estrogen and low testosterone, which is exactly why a comprehensive hormone panel — including DHEA-S, the sulfated storage form of DHEA that's measured in blood — is so important. Many conventional providers skip this test. If yours hasn't ordered it, it's worth asking.

## The Research Behind DHEA Benefits in Menopause

The science on DHEA benefits in menopause has matured considerably over the last two decades, and the findings are worth knowing.

The most clinically validated use of DHEA in menopausal women is for genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM) — the collection of symptoms including vaginal dryness, irritation, pain during intercourse, and urinary symptoms that affect an estimated 50% or more of postmenopausal women. Research published in peer-reviewed journals and reviewed by the North American Menopause Society (NAMS) supports the use of intravaginal DHEA (prasterone), which was FDA-approved in 2016 under the brand name Intrarosa. Multiple randomized controlled trials demonstrated significant improvements in vaginal tissue health, lubrication, and sexual pain compared to placebo.

Beyond genitourinary symptoms, research suggests broader potential benefits. A review of multiple studies found associations between higher DHEA levels and better cognitive function, improved mood, and maintained muscle mass in older women. Some research published in peer-reviewed endocrinology journals has explored DHEA's role in bone density preservation, with promising but still evolving findings.

It's important to distinguish between FDA-approved intravaginal DHEA, which has a robust clinical evidence base, and over-the-counter oral DHEA supplements, which are less regulated and have a more mixed research record. Oral DHEA supplementation does show benefits in some studies — particularly for libido and well-being — but dosing, absorption, and conversion rates vary significantly between individuals, making professional oversight essential.

## DHEA and the Stress-Hormone Connection

One factor that accelerates DHEA decline in many women is chronic stress — and this is where the hormone story gets more complicated.

Both DHEA and cortisol are produced by the adrenal glands, and they share a biochemical relationship that can work against you under sustained stress. When the body prioritizes cortisol production to manage a chronic stress load, it can do so partly at the expense of DHEA synthesis — a phenomenon sometimes described informally as "cortisol steal" or "pregnenolone steal." While the full mechanism is still debated in the research community, the net effect is that women under significant long-term stress often show lower DHEA levels alongside elevated cortisol.

This matters because so many women in their 40s and 50s are managing enormous stress loads — caregiving, career demands, life transitions — right at the moment their DHEA is naturally declining anyway. The practical takeaway: optimizing DHEA isn't just about supplementation. It also means addressing the lifestyle and stress factors that may be accelerating adrenal depletion in the first place.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does DHEA do for women?

DHEA is a precursor hormone produced by the adrenal glands that the body converts into estrogen and testosterone. In women, it supports energy, libido, mood, bone density, and immune function. Levels peak in your mid-20s and decline steadily with age, which is why many women in perimenopause and menopause notice its effects most acutely.

### Should women take DHEA during menopause?

Some women and their providers consider DHEA supplementation during menopause to address symptoms like low libido, vaginal dryness, fatigue, and brain fog. Research, including studies on the prescription vaginal DHEA product Intrarosa, supports its use for specific symptoms. However, DHEA is not right for everyone and should only be used under the guidance of a qualified healthcare provider.

### What are the signs of low DHEA in women?

Signs of low DHEA in women can include persistent fatigue, decreased libido, thinning hair, dry skin, depressed mood, difficulty building muscle, and increased susceptibility to illness. These symptoms overlap with general hormonal decline, so lab testing — specifically a DHEA-S blood test — is the most reliable way to confirm whether low DHEA is a contributing factor.

### Does DHEA increase estrogen levels in women?

Yes, DHEA can be converted into estrogen (as well as testosterone) by peripheral tissues throughout the body. This is one reason DHEA is considered a prohormone — it does not act directly as estrogen but supplies the raw material your body uses to produce it. The conversion rate varies by individual, which is why professional monitoring is important.

## Common Questions About DHEA

### What's the difference between DHEA and DHEA-S?

DHEA and DHEA-S (DHEA-sulfate) are two forms of the same hormone. DHEA-S is the sulfated, storage form that circulates in the bloodstream in much higher concentrations and has a longer half-life. When your provider orders a DHEA blood test, they are almost always measuring DHEA-S because it gives a more stable and accurate picture of your adrenal hormone output.

### Can I just buy DHEA at the health food store?

DHEA is available as an over-the-counter supplement in the United States, unlike in most other countries where it requires a prescription. However, OTC supplements vary widely in quality, purity, and actual hormone content. Taking DHEA without knowing your baseline levels or how you metabolize it can lead to hormonal imbalances — including excess androgens — so professional guidance and testing are strongly recommended before starting any regimen.

### How do I get my DHEA levels tested?

Ask your primary care provider, gynecologist, or a functional medicine or integrative health practitioner to order a DHEA-S blood test. It is a standard lab test that most major labs process. Results are interpreted in the context of your age, symptoms, and other hormone levels — a single number in isolation doesn't tell the full story, so work with a provider who looks at the whole picture.

### Is DHEA safe for women with a history of hormone-sensitive cancer?

Women with a personal history of hormone-sensitive cancers — such as estrogen receptor-positive breast cancer — should discuss DHEA carefully with their oncologist before considering any supplementation. Because DHEA converts to estrogen and testosterone in the body, there are legitimate safety questions that require individualized medical evaluation. This is not an area for self-experimentation.

### How long does it take to feel a difference after starting DHEA?

Many patients report that noticeable improvements in energy, mood, and libido begin within four to eight weeks of starting DHEA therapy, though this varies depending on the dose, form (oral vs. vaginal), and individual metabolism. Full effects on vaginal tissue health and bone markers may take three to six months to assess meaningfully. Regular follow-up testing is important to confirm the therapy is working and to adjust dosing as needed.
