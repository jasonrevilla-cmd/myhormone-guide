# EvexiPEL vs BioTE vs SottoPelle — The Complete Pellet System Comparison

If you've been researching pellet therapy, you've almost certainly run into three names: EvexiPEL, BioTE, and SottoPelle. The comparison of evexipel vs biote vs sottopelle comes up constantly in patient forums, and for good reason — these are the three dominant physician training and pellet delivery systems in the United States, and choosing between them isn't as simple as picking a brand of vitamins. The system your provider uses shapes how your dose is calculated, what labs they run, how often you return for re-insertion, and ultimately how well your symptoms resolve.

Here's the truth mainstream medicine rarely admits: not all pellet therapy is created equal. The pellets themselves — small, rice-grain-sized cylinders of bioidentical testosterone or estradiol — are relatively similar across systems. What differs significantly is the clinical philosophy behind them, the training your provider received, the dosing algorithm guiding your prescription, and the overall patient experience. This guide breaks down each system so you can walk into any provider conversation fully informed.

## What Is Pellet Therapy — And Why Does the System Behind It Matter?

Pellet therapy delivers bioidentical hormones through small compressed pellets inserted subcutaneously, typically in the upper buttock. If you want a thorough primer on the mechanics, our guide on [what hormone pellet therapy is and how it works](/what-is-hormone-pellet-therapy) covers the fundamentals in detail.

The reason the system matters is this: the pellet itself is compounded by a licensed compounding pharmacy and is broadly similar regardless of which training network your provider belongs to. What differs is everything surrounding the pellet — how your provider was trained, what dosing formula they use, what labs they order, how they monitor your response, and what supplemental protocols they may recommend. A poorly trained provider using any system will produce worse outcomes than a skilled, experienced provider using a competing one. That said, the systems themselves carry real philosophical differences worth understanding.

## BioTE — The Largest Network and What That Means for Patients

BioTE is the most widely recognized pellet therapy system in the United States, with thousands of certified providers across the country. The network's scale is its primary strength: if you live in a mid-sized city, there is almost certainly a BioTE-certified provider nearby.

BioTE providers complete a structured training program covering pellet insertion technique, lab interpretation, and the company's proprietary dosing guidelines. The system uses bloodwork-based dosing — primarily total testosterone, free testosterone, estradiol, SHBG, and a standard metabolic panel — to determine starting doses. BioTE also has a strong emphasis on nutraceutical supplementation, and many BioTE providers will recommend the company's branded supplement line alongside pellet therapy. For some patients, this is a welcome add-on; for others, it feels like upselling.

The most commonly cited patient concern with BioTE isn't the pellets — it's provider variability. Because the network is large, quality ranges considerably. Some BioTE providers are deeply attentive clinicians who fine-tune dosing aggressively; others run high-volume practices where patients feel rushed. If you're evaluating a BioTE provider specifically, our detailed breakdown of [BioTE pellet therapy — how it works, costs, and what to expect](/biote-pellet-therapy-how-it-works-costs-and-what-to-expect) will help you ask the right questions before your first appointment.

**Key characteristics of BioTE:**
- Largest provider network in the U.S.
- Structured training program with ongoing education requirements
- Bloodwork-driven dosing protocol
- Strong nutraceutical supplement program (optional but often recommended)
- Provider quality varies — volume and experience matter

## EvexiPEL — The Optimization-Focused Challenger

EvexiPEL positions itself as a more clinically rigorous alternative, with an emphasis on comprehensive hormone optimization rather than symptom management alone. The system was developed by practitioners who wanted a protocol that looked beyond testosterone and estradiol in isolation and considered the full hormonal ecosystem — including thyroid, adrenal function, and metabolic markers.

EvexiPEL-trained providers typically run broader initial lab panels than what many BioTE providers order as standard, and the training program places heavier emphasis on interpreting complex hormone interactions. The philosophy leans toward what integrative medicine calls "optimization" — not just getting hormones into a normal reference range, but targeting levels associated with peak vitality and symptom resolution.

In the biote vs evexipel conversation, patients who have tried both often describe EvexiPEL providers as more willing to adjust dosing proactively and to consider hormones like DHEA, pregnenolone, and thyroid alongside the core sex hormones. This can be a meaningful advantage for patients whose symptoms haven't fully resolved on testosterone and estradiol alone.

The trade-off is availability. EvexiPEL's network, while growing, is smaller than BioTE's. In rural or smaller metro areas, finding an EvexiPEL-certified provider may require more searching — or telehealth combined with a local insertion provider.

**Key characteristics of EvexiPEL:**
- Optimization-focused clinical philosophy
- Broader hormone panel emphasis
- Smaller but growing provider network
- Strong reputation in integrative and functional medicine circles
- May involve more comprehensive initial workup

## SottoPelle — Proprietary Algorithm and a Longer Track Record

SottoPelle is the oldest of the three systems, founded by Dr. Gino Tutera in the 1990s. It holds a distinctive position in the pellet therapy brands compared conversation because it uses a proprietary dosing algorithm — the SottoPelle Method — that calculates dose based on weight, symptom severity, activity level, and lab values simultaneously, rather than relying on labs alone.

The algorithm-driven approach is SottoPelle's defining feature and its most debated one. Proponents argue it produces more individualized dosing because it factors in the whole patient, not just a single lab number. Critics point out that an algorithm is only as good as the inputs — and that it can reduce the provider's flexibility to deviate from the formula based on clinical judgment.

SottoPelle providers tend to be found in specialty wellness clinics and integrative practices. The training is rigorous, and the company maintains strict certification standards. Many patients who have tried both sottopelle vs biote report that SottoPelle providers spend more time on the initial consultation — which translates to a higher perceived level of personalization, even if the underlying pellets are biochemically similar.

One practical note: SottoPelle's provider network is smaller than BioTE's but comparable in geographic spread to EvexiPEL. Expect to do some searching if you're in a less populated area.

**Key characteristics of SottoPelle:**
- Longest track record of the three systems
- Proprietary multi-variable dosing algorithm
- Algorithm factors in weight, symptoms, activity level, and labs
- Rigorous provider certification
- Smaller network; typically found in specialty wellness clinics

## How to Choose the Right System — The Questions That Actually Matter

The best pellet therapy system is the one delivered by a skilled, engaged provider who individualizes your care. Research published in *Maturitas* and other peer-reviewed menopause journals consistently shows that outcomes in hormone therapy are more closely tied to provider competency and individualized dosing than to the specific delivery mechanism. With that as the baseline, here's how to evaluate your options practically.

**Ask every candidate provider:**
1. How many pellet insertions do you perform per month? (Higher volume generally means greater technical skill.)
2. What labs do you run before my first insertion — and at follow-up?
3. How do you adjust dosing if my symptoms don't fully resolve after the first cycle?
4. What is your protocol if I experience side effects like elevated hematocrit or androgenic symptoms?
5. Do you look at thyroid and adrenal markers, or only sex hormones?

A provider who answers these questions confidently, without rushing you, and who acknowledges that optimization sometimes takes two or three cycles to dial in — that is a better indicator of likely success than which training banner hangs on their wall.

According to the North American Menopause Society (NAMS), individualized hormone therapy that accounts for a patient's full symptom picture, health history, and risk profile produces better outcomes than standardized dosing. That principle applies whether your provider trained under BioTE, EvexiPEL, SottoPelle, or any other system.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between EvexiPEL, BioTE, and SottoPelle?

EvexiPEL, BioTE, and SottoPelle are all physician training and pellet delivery systems for bioidentical hormone therapy, but they differ in dosing philosophy, compounding partnerships, provider training requirements, and cost structures. BioTE is the largest network, SottoPelle uses a proprietary dosing algorithm, and EvexiPEL emphasizes optimization-focused protocols and broader hormone panels.

### Which pellet therapy system is best for women?

No single system is universally best for women — the right choice depends on your provider's experience, the dosing flexibility they offer, and how aggressively they pursue symptom resolution. Many women report excellent results with all three systems when paired with an attentive, experienced provider who individualizes dosing rather than relying solely on standardized formulas.

### Is BioTE or EvexiPEL more expensive?

Costs vary by geographic region and individual provider pricing rather than the brand itself. Generally, pellet insertion procedures range from $300–$650 per session for women and $600–$900 for men across all three systems. BioTE providers may charge additional fees for supplement protocols. Always request a full cost breakdown from your specific provider before committing.

### How do I find a provider trained in EvexiPEL, BioTE, or SottoPelle?

Each system maintains a provider directory on its official website. BioTE, EvexiPEL, and SottoPelle all offer searchable locators where you can find certified practitioners in your area. It is worth calling ahead to ask how many pellet insertions the provider performs monthly — volume correlates strongly with technical proficiency and dosing experience.

### Can I switch from one pellet system to another?

Yes, you can switch pellet therapy systems, though your new provider will likely want to review your hormone labs before your first insertion with them. Since pellet compositions and dosing formulas differ between systems, your new provider should not simply replicate the prior dose — they should start fresh with updated bloodwork and a full symptom assessment.

## Common Questions About Pellet Therapy Brands

### Which is better — BioTE or SottoPelle?

Neither system is objectively superior — both train providers in bioidentical pellet therapy and both produce satisfied patients. The more important variable is the individual provider's experience and willingness to adjust your dosing over time. BioTE has a larger network, making it easier to find a local provider; SottoPelle uses a multi-variable algorithm some patients find more personalized.

### How long do hormone pellets last from any of these systems?

Pellets from BioTE, EvexiPEL, and SottoPelle all follow the same general pharmacokinetic curve. Women typically need re-insertion every 3–5 months, and men every 4–6 months. Activity level, metabolism, and starting dose all influence how quickly the pellet is absorbed, regardless of which system was used.

### Are hormone pellets FDA approved?

The individual hormones used in pellets — testosterone and estradiol — are FDA-approved compounds. However, the compounded pellet preparations themselves are not FDA-approved drug products; they are compounded by licensed pharmacies under state pharmacy board oversight. This applies equally to BioTE, EvexiPEL, SottoPelle, and all other pellet systems.

### What side effects should I watch for with any pellet system?

The most commonly reported side effects across all pellet systems include temporary bruising or soreness at the insertion site, and in some cases, androgenic effects such as acne, increased body hair, or elevated red blood cell count (hematocrit) if testosterone dosing is too high. A good provider monitors labs at follow-up visits to catch and correct these early.

### Do I need to go to a specific brand's provider, or can any doctor insert pellets?

Pellet insertion is a minor in-office procedure, but training matters. Providers certified through BioTE, EvexiPEL, or SottoPelle have completed structured programs covering technique and dosing protocols. While any licensed physician could theoretically insert a pellet, choosing a provider trained through one of these established systems — and with meaningful insertion volume — significantly reduces technical risk and improves dosing accuracy.
