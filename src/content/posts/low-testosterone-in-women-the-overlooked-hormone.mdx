# Low Testosterone in Women: The Overlooked Hormone

If you've been feeling exhausted no matter how much you sleep, struggling to stay focused, or noticing that your drive — in the bedroom and elsewhere — has quietly disappeared, low testosterone in women may be part of the answer no one has given you yet. Testosterone is almost universally discussed as a male hormone, which means millions of women are living with a treatable deficiency that goes unnamed, untested, and unaddressed. You are not imagining your symptoms. And you are not alone.

This post will walk you through exactly what female low testosterone symptoms look like, why this deficiency is so commonly missed, what the science actually says, and what your options are for getting back to feeling like yourself. Consider this your starting point.

## Why Low Testosterone in Women Is So Frequently Missed

Here's an uncomfortable truth: conventional medicine has historically focused testosterone research almost entirely on men. As a result, many physicians were never trained to recognize testosterone deficiency in female patients, let alone test for it or treat it. When women bring up symptoms like fatigue, flat mood, or low libido, they are far more likely to be handed an antidepressant or told that what they're experiencing is simply part of aging.

Meanwhile, testosterone is anything but irrelevant to women. Throughout a woman's reproductive life, the ovaries and adrenal glands produce testosterone in quantities that, while smaller than in men, are physiologically significant. According to research published in the *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism*, testosterone plays a direct role in energy metabolism, bone density, muscle maintenance, mood regulation, cognitive function, and sexual desire in women. Levels begin declining gradually in a woman's 20s and can drop sharply after surgical menopause or during perimenopause.

The result is a gap: real symptoms, a measurable deficiency, and a medical system that hasn't yet caught up. Understanding this context is the first step toward advocating for yourself.

## Female Low Testosterone Symptoms: What to Watch For

Testosterone deficiency in women rarely announces itself dramatically. Instead, it tends to arrive as a slow erosion of the qualities that define your vitality. Many women describe it as feeling like a dimmer switch has gradually been turned down — and they can't figure out why.

The most commonly reported female low testosterone symptoms include:

- **Persistent, unexplained fatigue** that sleep doesn't fix
- **Reduced or absent libido** — a loss of interest in sex that feels out of character
- **Difficulty building or maintaining muscle**, even with regular exercise
- **Increased body fat**, particularly around the abdomen
- **Mood changes** including low-grade depression, anxiety, or emotional flatness
- **Brain fog** — trouble concentrating, slower recall, mental cloudiness
- **Decreased motivation and drive**, both professionally and personally
- **Thinning hair** or changes in skin quality
- **Reduced bone density**, increasing osteoporosis risk over time

What makes diagnosis tricky is that many of these symptoms overlap with estrogen deficiency, thyroid dysfunction, depression, and general stress. That's precisely why comprehensive hormone testing matters — symptoms point the direction, but labs confirm the destination. ## What Causes Testosterone Deficiency in Women?

Understanding why testosterone levels fall helps clarify both your symptoms and your options. The causes are varied, and more than one can be present at the same time.

**Natural aging** is the most common driver. Testosterone production peaks in a woman's mid-20s and declines by roughly 50% by the time she reaches her 40s. This decline is gradual and often goes unnoticed until it crosses a personal threshold.

**Surgical menopause** — the removal of both ovaries — causes an abrupt and dramatic drop in testosterone because the ovaries are responsible for producing roughly 25% of a woman's testosterone directly, and they signal production indirectly as well. Women who undergo oophorectomy often notice symptoms far more acutely than those who go through natural menopause.

**Oral contraceptives and certain hormone therapies** can raise levels of sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), a protein that binds to testosterone and renders it biologically inactive. Even if total testosterone appears normal on a lab report, elevated SHBG can mean very little free testosterone is actually available to your cells.

**Adrenal insufficiency** disrupts production of DHEA, a precursor hormone that the body converts into testosterone.

**Chronic stress, poor sleep, and certain medications** — including opioids and some antidepressants — can also suppress testosterone output over time.

## How Testosterone Deficiency in Women Is Diagnosed

Testing for low testosterone is straightforward in concept, though interpretation requires nuance. A morning blood draw — when testosterone levels are naturally at their highest — should measure both total testosterone and free testosterone. Free testosterone is particularly important because it reflects the amount actually available for biological use rather than the amount bound and inactive.

Your provider may also test:

- **SHBG** — to understand how much testosterone is being bound
- **DHEA-S** — to assess adrenal contribution to testosterone production
- **Estradiol and progesterone** — because hormones don't operate in isolation
- **Thyroid panel** — since thyroid dysfunction can mimic and compound testosterone deficiency

One significant challenge is that no universally accepted reference range for female testosterone exists. The Endocrine Society has acknowledged this gap, noting that the absence of standardized normative data makes diagnosis and management more complex for women than for men. This is why symptoms must be weighed alongside lab values — a number in the "normal" range on a generic lab reference scale may still be too low for *you* as an individual.

A skilled hormone-literate provider will treat the patient in front of them, not just a number on a printout.

## Low T Women Treatment: What Are Your Options?

Because no testosterone product has received FDA approval specifically for use in women in the United States, all treatments are prescribed off-label. This does not mean they are unsafe or experimental — off-label prescribing is common, legal, and often supported by robust clinical evidence. It simply means the formal approval pathway hasn't been pursued for the female indication.

Current treatment approaches include:

**Topical testosterone cream or gel** — A small, precisely dosed amount is applied daily to the skin (often the inner arm or thigh). This is the most flexible option and allows for easy dose adjustments. Compounding pharmacies prepare most female-dose testosterone this way.

**Subcutaneous testosterone pellets** — Tiny pellets about the size of a grain of rice are inserted under the skin, typically in the hip or buttock area, and release testosterone steadily over three to six months. Many women prefer this option for its consistency and convenience. If you want to understand how this works in detail, [Testosterone Pellet Therapy for Women: Benefits and What to Expect](/testosterone-pellet-therapy-for-women-benefits-and-what-to-expect) covers the process thoroughly.

**Low-dose testosterone patches** — Available in some countries outside the United States, patches like Intrinsa have demonstrated efficacy in clinical trials for hypoactive sexual desire disorder in postmenopausal women.

Regardless of delivery method, appropriate treatment should be accompanied by baseline labs, follow-up testing at regular intervals, and ongoing communication with your provider about symptoms and response.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are the symptoms of low testosterone in women?

Common symptoms of low testosterone in women include persistent fatigue, reduced sex drive, difficulty building or maintaining muscle mass, unexplained weight gain, depression, anxiety, poor concentration, and brain fog. Some women also report thinning hair, dry skin, and a general loss of motivation or sense of well-being. These symptoms often overlap with other hormonal imbalances, which is why testing is essential for an accurate diagnosis.

### What causes low testosterone in women?

Low testosterone in women can result from natural aging, surgical menopause (removal of the ovaries), adrenal insufficiency, use of oral contraceptives or hormone therapy that raises sex hormone-binding globulin, and certain chronic illnesses. The ovaries and adrenal glands are the primary producers of testosterone in women, so anything affecting these organs can reduce output. Chronic stress and poor sleep can also suppress testosterone production over time.

### How is low testosterone diagnosed in women?

Diagnosis typically involves a blood test measuring total and free testosterone levels, ideally drawn in the morning when levels are highest. Because no universally agreed-upon reference range for female testosterone exists, results must be interpreted alongside symptoms and clinical history. Many providers also test DHEA-S, sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), and other hormones to get a complete picture of overall hormonal health.

### What treatments are available for low testosterone in women?

Treatment options include topical testosterone creams or gels applied to the skin, subcutaneous testosterone pellets, and in some countries low-dose testosterone patches. In the United States, no testosterone product is currently FDA-approved specifically for women, so these therapies are prescribed off-label by trained providers. Treatment decisions should always be individualized and monitored with regular follow-up lab work.
