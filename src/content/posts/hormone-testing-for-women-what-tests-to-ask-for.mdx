# Hormone Testing for Women: What Tests to Ask For — and Why Most Panels Fall Short

You've sat in the exam room, described months of debilitating fatigue, weight gain that won't budge, sleep that never refreshes you, and a libido that vanished. Your doctor ran "hormone tests." Results came back "normal." And you left with no answers and a creeping suspicion that something important was missed. You are not wrong. Hormone testing for women is one of the most underutilized and poorly standardized areas of routine medicine — and knowing exactly which tests to request can fundamentally change the conversation with your provider.

This guide breaks down every key test in a comprehensive women's hormone blood panel, explains what each marker reveals, and gives you the language to advocate for yourself at your next appointment. Whether you are in your early 40s noticing subtle shifts or deep in the symptoms of menopause, understanding your hormones starts with knowing what to measure.

## Why Standard Hormone Testing for Women Often Misses the Mark

Standard hormone testing for women frequently stops at one or two markers — usually TSH (thyroid) and sometimes FSH — and calls it a day. That approach fails the majority of symptomatic women for a straightforward reason: hormones do not work in isolation. Estrogen, progesterone, testosterone, cortisol, DHEA, and thyroid hormones are part of an interconnected system. A deficiency or imbalance in one area can ripple through all the others, producing symptoms that look like depression, thyroid disease, or simple aging — when the real driver is hormonal.

Mainstream medicine has also historically underestimated the hormonal complexity of perimenopause, the 2 to 10-year transition before the final menstrual period. During this phase, estrogen and progesterone levels fluctuate erratically rather than declining in a straight line. A single blood draw on the wrong day of the cycle can show "normal" estradiol even in a woman who is genuinely struggling. This is why a full hormone panel, timed correctly and interpreted in clinical context, is the only approach that delivers actionable information.

Before you read further, it helps to understand the basic roles these hormones play. ## The Core Hormone Panel: What to Request

A well-constructed women's hormone blood test goes well beyond a single number. Below are the markers that provide a meaningful clinical picture.

**Estradiol (E2)** is the most potent and clinically relevant form of estrogen in reproductive-age and perimenopausal women. It governs mood, bone density, cardiovascular health, cognitive function, and vaginal tissue integrity. For menstruating women, this test is most informative on cycle day 3 (follicular baseline). Postmenopausal reference ranges typically fall below 30 pg/mL, though optimal ranges for symptom relief in women on therapy are debated among providers.

**Progesterone** works in balance with estrogen. Low progesterone relative to estrogen — even when estrogen itself is not elevated — can cause anxiety, sleep disruption, heavy periods, and fluid retention. This test should be drawn on cycle day 19 to 21 in menstruating women; postmenopausal women can test on any day.

**FSH (Follicle-Stimulating Hormone)** rises as ovarian reserve declines. An FSH above 10 mIU/mL — especially if trending upward across multiple tests — is a clinically significant marker for perimenopause. In confirmed menopause, FSH typically exceeds 30 to 40 mIU/mL.

**LH (Luteinizing Hormone)** is most useful in tandem with FSH. An elevated LH:FSH ratio can indicate polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS) in younger women; in older women, elevated LH alongside rising FSH confirms ovarian decline.

**Total and Free Testosterone** — yes, women need testosterone. It drives energy, libido, muscle tone, mood, and cognitive sharpness. Many women in perimenopause experience testosterone decline before estrogen becomes a problem. Both total and free (bioavailable) testosterone should be measured, since SHBG levels affect how much testosterone is actually usable.

**SHBG (Sex Hormone-Binding Globulin)** binds to testosterone and estrogen, making them unavailable to tissue. High SHBG — often driven by oral estrogen or high-carbohydrate diets — can cause low-testosterone symptoms even when total testosterone looks adequate on paper.

**DHEA-S** is an adrenal precursor hormone that converts into testosterone and estrogen. Declining DHEA-S is associated with fatigue, low libido, and reduced stress resilience. It is a reliable, stable marker that can be drawn at any time of day.

## Thyroid and Adrenal Markers: The Hormones Providers Forget to Mention

Thyroid and adrenal testing should be part of any comprehensive hormone panel for women, because their symptoms overlap almost completely with estrogen and progesterone imbalance.

**TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone)** is the standard thyroid screen, but it is not sufficient on its own. Many women with clear hypothyroid symptoms — fatigue, weight gain, hair loss, cold intolerance — have TSH values within the "normal" lab range (typically 0.5 to 4.5 mIU/L) while their tissue-level thyroid function is suboptimal. Functional medicine providers often aim for TSH between 1.0 and 2.0 mIU/L in symptomatic women.

**Free T3 and Free T4** measure the active and precursor forms of thyroid hormone. Free T3 is the metabolically active form that cells actually use. A woman can have normal TSH and T4 but poor T4-to-T3 conversion — a common issue after 40 that a TSH-only test will entirely miss.

**Fasting Cortisol** (morning draw) reflects adrenal output. Chronic stress, poor sleep, and perimenopausal hormonal shifts can dysregulate the HPA axis, resulting in cortisol patterns — either abnormally high or "burned out" low — that drive fatigue, belly fat accumulation, and mood instability. Providers may also recommend a 4-point salivary cortisol test for a more complete diurnal picture.

**Insulin and Fasting Glucose** are worth including because insulin resistance frequently develops during perimenopause and worsens hormonal symptoms across the board. Research published in *Menopause* has linked estrogen decline to increasing insulin resistance, making metabolic markers a clinically relevant addition to any perimenopausal panel.

## Estrogen Progesterone Testing: Timing Is Everything

Estrogen progesterone testing produces meaningless or misleading results if the blood draw is not timed to your cycle. This is one of the most common reasons women are told their hormones are "normal" when they are anything but.

For women who are still menstruating — even irregularly:

- **Day 3 of your cycle** (day 1 = first full day of bleeding): Test FSH, LH, and estradiol. This gives your follicular baseline and screens for diminished ovarian reserve.
- **Day 19 to 21** (mid-luteal phase, assuming a 28-day cycle): Test progesterone. This is when progesterone should peak. A result below 5 ng/mL mid-luteal strongly suggests inadequate progesterone production, even if the cycle appears regular.

For women who are postmenopausal or not menstruating, timing matters less for estradiol and progesterone — but cortisol should still be drawn in the morning, and testosterone is generally highest in the early morning hours.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What hormone tests should a woman ask for?

A comprehensive hormone panel for women should include estradiol (E2), progesterone, FSH, LH, total and free testosterone, DHEA-S, SHBG, and thyroid markers (TSH, free T3, free T4). Fasting cortisol is also worth requesting if fatigue or sleep disruption is a major symptom. Many primary care providers run only TSH and FSH, which is rarely enough to build a full clinical picture. Bringing a written list of requested tests to your appointment — and citing your specific symptoms — significantly improves the likelihood of getting a complete panel ordered.

### What does a hormone blood test show in women?

A women's hormone blood test measures circulating levels of key hormones — estrogen, progesterone, testosterone, and others — to identify deficiencies, imbalances, or patterns consistent with perimenopause or menopause. Results help a provider determine whether symptoms like fatigue, mood swings, low libido, or irregular periods have a hormonal root cause and which, if any, interventions may be appropriate. The results are most useful when interpreted alongside symptom history rather than in isolation against a lab reference range.

### What is a normal estrogen level for a woman?

Normal estrogen (estradiol) levels vary significantly by cycle phase and life stage. In premenopausal women, estradiol typically ranges from about 30 to 400 pg/mL depending on the phase of the cycle. In postmenopausal women, levels generally fall below 30 pg/mL. Your provider should interpret your result in the context of your symptoms, age, and cycle day — a single number without context is rarely the whole story, and "within range" does not always mean optimal for your individual biology.

### Can hormone testing tell you if you are in perimenopause?

Hormone testing can support a perimenopause diagnosis, but no single test confirms it definitively. Elevated FSH (typically above 10 mIU/mL, trending higher over time), fluctuating estradiol, and irregular progesterone patterns are common markers. Because hormone levels fluctuate dramatically during perimenopause, providers often look at symptom history alongside multiple lab values rather than relying on one blood draw. The North American Menopause Society notes that perimenopause is primarily a clinical diagnosis supported — but not replaced — by laboratory data.

### How often should women get hormone levels checked?

For women actively managing symptoms or on hormone therapy, most providers recommend checking hormone levels every 3 to 6 months initially, then annually once levels are stable. Women in early perimenopause with mild symptoms may test once a year as a baseline. The right frequency depends on your symptoms, age, and whether you are on any form of hormone therapy. If you are preparing for your first BHRT evaluation, [What Happens at Your First BHRT Consultation?](/what-happens-at-your-first-bhrt-consultation) explains exactly how providers use your lab results in that initial appointment.

### Does time of day affect hormone test results?

Yes — significantly. Cortisol should be tested in the morning when levels peak. For menstruating women, estradiol and progesterone results are most meaningful when timed to specific cycle days (typically day 3 for FSH and estradiol; day 19 to 21 for progesterone). Testosterone is also generally highest in the morning. Scheduling your draw correctly and noting where you are in your cycle on the lab form can make the difference between useful data and misleading numbers.

## Common Questions About Hormone Testing for Women

### What's the difference between total testosterone and free testosterone for women?

Total testosterone measures all testosterone in your blood, including what's bound to proteins like SHBG. Free testosterone measures only the portion available for your cells to actually use. A woman can have adequate total testosterone but very low free testosterone — and experience all the symptoms of deficiency — if her SHBG is high. Both numbers together give a far more useful picture than either alone.

### Can I get hormone testing done without a doctor's order?

In many U.S. states, direct-to-consumer lab services allow you to order your own blood panels without a physician's referral. Companies like Ulta Lab Tests and LabCorp's direct access program offer hormone panels that you can order online and have drawn at a local lab. However, interpreting results without clinical context is genuinely difficult — ideally, you use direct-access testing to arrive at a provider appointment already informed, not to self-diagnose or self-treat.

### Why did my doctor say my hormones are "normal" when I still feel terrible?

Standard lab reference ranges are built from large population averages and define "normal" as what most people have — not what is optimal for your individual biology or what correlates with feeling well. A progesterone level of 2 ng/mL might be technically within range but inadequate for symptom relief. Many women find that symptoms resolve only when their levels are optimized to the upper-mid range, not simply pulled out of the "low" flag zone. This is why working with a provider experienced in hormonal health matters.

### Is saliva or urine testing better than blood testing for hormones?

Blood (serum) testing is the most established and widely accepted method and is the standard used in most published research. Saliva testing can be useful for measuring free cortisol patterns throughout the day, and some functional medicine providers use Dutch (dried urine) testing for a more detailed view of hormone metabolites. Each method has legitimate uses, but serum is generally the most reliable starting point and the easiest to have interpreted by any provider.

### Should I stop birth control before getting hormone testing done?

Yes — if possible and medically appropriate for you. Hormonal contraceptives suppress your natural estrogen, progesterone, LH, and FSH, and significantly elevate SHBG. Testing while on hormonal birth control will not reflect your true hormonal status. Most providers recommend waiting at least one to three full cycles after stopping hormonal contraception before testing baseline levels. Always discuss this with your prescribing provider before making any changes.
