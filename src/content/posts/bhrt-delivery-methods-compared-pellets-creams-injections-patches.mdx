# BHRT Delivery Methods Compared: Pellets, Creams, Injections, and Patches

You've done the research. You know your symptoms are real — the fatigue that sleep doesn't fix, the brain fog rolling in mid-sentence, the weight that appeared out of nowhere and refuses to leave. You've heard that BHRT delivery methods vary significantly, and now you're wondering: does it actually matter *how* bioidentical hormones get into your body? The short answer is yes — more than most people realize. The delivery method affects how stable your hormone levels stay, how much daily effort is involved, and ultimately how well you feel.

This guide breaks down every major BHRT delivery method — pellets, creams, injections, and patches — with honest comparisons of how each works, who tends to do best with each option, and what the research says about their real-world performance. By the end, you'll have a clear framework for having a more informed conversation with your provider. ## Understanding Why BHRT Delivery Methods Matter

Before comparing specific formats, it helps to understand what's actually at stake when hormones are delivered differently.

Every BHRT delivery method follows the same biological goal: get a specific hormone — estradiol, progesterone, testosterone, or DHEA — into your bloodstream at a dose that restores balance without creating excess. But the path each method takes to the bloodstream is different, and that path has consequences.

**Absorption rate** determines how quickly hormone levels rise after each dose. **Consistency** determines whether your levels stay stable over time or spike and valley. **Bioavailability** — the percentage of the administered dose that actually reaches systemic circulation — varies meaningfully between delivery routes.

These aren't trivial details. Hormone level fluctuations are directly linked to symptom recurrence. Many patients who report that "BHRT stopped working" are actually experiencing the trough phase of an inconsistent delivery method rather than a true resistance to therapy. Research published in clinical endocrinology literature consistently shows that stable hormone delivery correlates with more sustained symptom improvement compared to methods that produce peaks and troughs.

The other factor is practicality. A delivery method you won't use consistently — because it requires daily application, causes skin irritation, or demands frequent clinic visits — is less effective in the real world than one that fits your life. Both clinical efficacy and lifestyle fit matter when choosing between the types of BHRT available today.

## Hormone Pellets: The Set-It-and-Forget-It Option

Pellet therapy has grown significantly in popularity over the past decade, and for many patients, it has become the preferred BHRT delivery method among the options available.

Here's how it works: A compounding pharmacy creates small, cylindrical pellets — roughly the size of a grain of rice — containing a precisely calibrated dose of bioidentical hormones, most commonly testosterone and/or estradiol. A provider makes a small incision in the upper buttock area or hip and inserts the pellets just beneath the skin under local anesthesia. The procedure takes about 10–15 minutes and requires no stitches.

Once inserted, the pellets dissolve gradually, releasing hormones in response to your body's cardiac output — meaning physical activity naturally increases delivery rate, mimicking the body's own feedback mechanisms to some degree. Most patients need re-insertion every three to six months, depending on their metabolism and symptom profile.

The primary clinical argument for pellets is consistency. Because there's no daily dosing, there's no human error in the equation — and no peaks and crashes tied to application schedules. Studies examining pellet therapy have found that patients tend to maintain stable serum hormone levels throughout the dosing interval, which many providers and patients associate with more sustained symptom relief.

For an in-depth look at why this matters physiologically, see our article on [Why Pellets Deliver More Consistent Hormone Levels](/pellets-consistent-hormone-levels).

**Pellets are often a strong fit for:** patients who want a low-maintenance solution, those who have had inconsistent results with other delivery formats, and active individuals whose hormone needs fluctuate with physical exertion.

**Potential considerations:** Dosing cannot be adjusted once the pellet is inserted. If a dose is too high, you wait for it to metabolize. Finding an experienced pellet provider matters significantly, as proper dosing and insertion technique are essential.

## Topical Creams and Gels: Flexibility With Trade-Offs

Compounded bioidentical hormone creams and gels applied to the skin represent one of the most widely used BHRT delivery methods, particularly for women using progesterone and low-dose estradiol.

The appeal is clear: you control the dose, application is simple, and adjustments can be made without a procedure. Creams are typically applied to thin-skinned areas — inner wrists, inner arms, inner thighs — once or twice daily. Because they're compounded, they can contain custom combinations of hormones in a single preparation.

The challenge is absorption variability. Research has consistently shown that transdermal absorption of compounded hormones is highly individual and difficult to predict. Skin thickness, hydration levels, application site, and even ambient temperature affect how much hormone actually crosses the skin barrier and enters circulation. Two people using the same dose of the same cream can end up with substantially different serum levels.

Progesterone creams have attracted particular scrutiny in the research literature. Studies have found that while topical progesterone does raise serum progesterone levels, the concentrations achieved are often lower than those produced by oral or vaginal routes — which matters for uterine protection in women using estrogen therapy.

That said, for patients with dose sensitivity who need fine-grained control — or for those who are early in their BHRT journey and still establishing the right dose — creams and gels offer a flexibility that pellets do not. Many providers use them as a starting point before transitioning patients to a more stable delivery format.

**Creams and gels are often a strong fit for:** patients who prefer self-administered therapy, those fine-tuning a new prescription, and those who want to avoid any type of procedure.

**Potential considerations:** Daily compliance is required, absorption variability can make dosing imprecise, and transfer to partners or children via skin contact is a documented concern that requires careful hygiene practices.

## Hormone Injections: High Potency, Requires Management

Injectable bioidentical hormones — most commonly testosterone cypionate or testosterone enanthate — deliver hormones directly into muscle or subcutaneous fat tissue, bypassing the skin barrier entirely. This produces very high bioavailability and a relatively rapid rise in serum hormone levels.

Injections are particularly common in male BHRT protocols, though they are also used in women requiring testosterone. Frequency varies from weekly to every two weeks, and increasingly, low-dose daily subcutaneous (just beneath the skin) injections are used to smooth out the level fluctuations associated with less frequent dosing.

The primary clinical advantage of injections is potency and predictability of absorption. Unlike transdermal methods, injections produce reliable, measurable serum levels that providers can use to dial in dosing with precision. For men with significantly low testosterone who need a robust response, injections often deliver that more efficiently than other formats.

The major trade-off is the peak-and-trough problem. Traditional intramuscular injections given every one to two weeks produce a supraphysiologic spike in the days immediately after injection, followed by a decline that can dip below optimal levels before the next dose. Many patients describe feeling great for a few days post-injection and then noticing a gradual return of fatigue or mood changes as levels drop. The shift to more frequent, lower-dose subcutaneous injections has addressed this for many patients.

**Injections are often a strong fit for:** men with significantly low testosterone, patients who need precise dose titration, and those who are comfortable with self-injection or have regular access to a clinic.

**Potential considerations:** Requires either self-injection training or regular provider visits, hormones must be kept refrigerated, and managing peak-and-trough fluctuations requires thoughtful dosing schedules.

## Hormone Patches: Steady Delivery, Skin-Deep Limitations

Transdermal patches are among the most-studied bioidentical hormone delivery options available, with FDA-approved estradiol patches backed by decades of clinical data. Patches are applied to clean, dry skin — typically the lower abdomen, buttocks, or upper arm — and changed every one to two weeks depending on the formulation.

The mechanism is a slow, controlled release of hormone through a membrane directly into the skin and bloodstream. This bypasses first-pass liver metabolism (unlike oral hormones), which is clinically significant: research, including data referenced in cardiovascular safety analyses of hormone therapy, suggests that transdermal estradiol carries a more favorable blood clot risk profile compared to oral estrogen formulations.

For estradiol replacement specifically, patches offer a well-established, reproducible delivery format that many gynecologists and menopause specialists consider a first-line option. The hormone release rate is consistent within a given formulation, making levels relatively predictable — though not quite as stable as pellets over longer intervals.

Skin irritation is the most commonly reported complaint. Redness, itching, and poor adhesion (especially in hot or humid climates or during exercise) are practical challenges that cause some patients to discontinue patches. Rotating application sites reduces irritation for most users.

Patches are not well-suited to testosterone delivery in most contexts, as available patch formulations are primarily designed for estradiol. Patients needing testosterone supplementation alongside estrogen typically require a second delivery method.

**Patches are often a strong fit for:** women replacing estradiol who want a non-daily, procedure-free option with a strong evidence base, and patients whose providers prefer FDA-approved delivery formats.

**Potential considerations:** Skin irritation and adhesion issues affect a meaningful percentage of users, testosterone is not available in patch form for most patients, and patches need to be disposed of carefully to prevent hormone exposure to children or pets.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Which BHRT delivery method is most effective?

Effectiveness depends on your individual hormone needs, lifestyle, and how your body absorbs hormones. Pellets are often favored for delivering stable, consistent levels without daily maintenance. Creams and patches work well for patients who need flexible dosing. Injections can be highly effective but require more frequent administration. Research suggests that steady hormone delivery — regardless of method — tends to produce the most consistent symptom relief. A qualified BHRT provider can help match the method to your physiology.

### What is the difference between bioidentical hormone pellets and creams?

Pellets are small, rice-sized implants inserted under the skin that release hormones gradually over three to six months, requiring no daily action. Creams are topical preparations applied to the skin daily, allowing for flexible dose adjustments. Pellets tend to produce more consistent blood levels, while creams offer easier dose modification. Absorption variability is a noted limitation of topical creams, as factors like skin thickness and application site can affect how much hormone actually enters the bloodstream.

### Are BHRT injections better than patches?

Neither is universally better — they suit different patients. Injections deliver hormones directly into muscle or fat tissue, producing relatively rapid absorption and strong initial effects, but hormone levels can fluctuate between doses. Patches provide a steady, controlled release through the skin over one to two weeks and are convenient for patients who prefer a set-and-forget approach. Patches can occasionally cause skin irritation. Your provider will consider your symptom pattern, hormone levels, and lifestyle before recommending one over the other.

### How do I know which BHRT delivery method is right for me?

The right method depends on several factors: your specific hormones being replaced, your daily routine, your sensitivity to fluctuating levels, your preference for self-administered versus provider-administered therapy, and your budget. Some patients do well starting with a flexible method like a cream or patch, then transitioning to pellets once their optimal dose is established. Others prefer pellets from the start for convenience. A thorough hormone panel and a consultation with an experienced BHRT provider are the essential first steps.

### Can you switch between BHRT delivery methods?

Yes, many patients switch delivery methods over time as their needs change or as they find one format works better for their body. Transitioning typically requires re-testing hormone levels and adjusting the dose for the new delivery format. It is important to work closely with a provider during any switch to avoid under- or over-dosing. Some patients use a combination of methods — for example, pellets for testosterone and a cream for progesterone — under provider supervision.
